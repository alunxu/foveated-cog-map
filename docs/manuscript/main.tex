\begin{abstract}
Hippocampal spatial coding reorganises with sensor structure across species, from foveated primates to echolocating bats and subterranean mole-rats; modern AI systems built on rich pretrained visual encoders systematically fail at fine-grained spatial reasoning despite strong surface recognition. Whether these observations share a mechanism is hard to test in either system: animal sensor ecology is fixed within species, and frontier visual--language and world-model systems are too architecturally complex to vary the encoder cleanly. We use a deep RL navigation agent as a controlled silicon model organism, varying only the visual sensor along an axis of encoder spatial bandwidth (none / $1{\times}1$ / $8{\times}8$ blurred / $8{\times}8$ uniform) while holding architecture, task, and training fixed. We report that spatial memory format in this setting reflects an emergent \emph{capacity allocation} between two routes carrying position information --- the visual encoder and the recurrent integration of GPS-sensor history --- with the share each route carries tracking encoder spatial bandwidth. The four conditions diverge systematically along the magnitude, format, and consumption axes of this allocation. Three concomitant signatures --- approximate preservation of position information under non-linear readout (linear $R^2$ range $\approx 2.0$ collapses to MLP range $\approx 0.5$, MINE total drops $\approx 1.5\times$), scene-invariance of the linear readout direction, and place-cell-like population structure --- are jointly consistent with this mechanism, connecting our setting to the invariance--disentanglement reading of the information bottleneck and to the rate vs.\ global-remapping distinction in hippocampal coding. This reframes sensor structure as a training-time lever on the format of artificial spatial memory --- a content-level alternative to training-distribution accounts of vision--language spatial-reasoning failures, and a falsifiable prediction for transformer-based world models that share the encoder--memory pipeline.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}

Visual perception and downstream memory in artificial systems are commonly treated as a pipeline: scale the encoder, and any downstream module benefits. Recent benchmarks complicate this picture. Vision--language models (VLMs) with state-of-the-art surface visual recognition still fail at fine-grained spatial reasoning~\citep{ramakrishnan2025space}. The default reading attributes this to a training-distribution gap. We pursue a structural reading instead: the encoder and the memory downstream of it are not separable modules but a co-trained system, and \emph{the encoder's structure shapes what is linearly readable from the memory about space} --- a content-level claim about the perception--memory interface, harder to test in frontier systems whose architectural complexity confounds encoder effects with everything else.

The question has biological precedent. Primate hippocampus encodes joint place--view--action state under foveated sampling~\citep{wirth2017gaze,rolls2024why}, while rodents with near-panoramic vision show classic place fields. The same effect appears within a single animal: bats produce non-overlapping hippocampal populations under vision vs.\ echolocation~\citep{geva2016remapping}, and congenitally blind humans develop enhanced hippocampal coupling on non-visual spatial tasks~\citep{kupers2014blind}. \emph{Sensor structure reshapes spatial memory.} We ask whether the same holds for artificial navigation agents.

Map-like representations emerge in the recurrent state of deep-RL navigation agents without being designed in, even in agents with no vision at all~\citep{wijmans2023emergence,banino2018grid,cueva2018grid}.\footnote{\small ``Cognitive map'' here means a linearly-decodable world-frame position code in the hidden state --- the operational quantity our probes measure. The broader-literature concept~\citep{epstein2017cognitive} (relational, hierarchical maps) is wider; richer non-linear structure may exist in the same hidden states but is outside our linear-probe scope.} This literature has typically reported one condition at a time --- a fully sighted or a fully blind agent --- leaving open how encoder structure interacts with what the memory builds. We use the same agent family as a \emph{comparative} chassis: identical LSTM backbone, task, and training, varying only the visual sensor across four conditions that span an axis of encoder spatial bandwidth (Figure~\ref{fig:setup}) --- no vision (\emph{blind}), a resolution-collapsed $1{\times}1$ encoder output (\emph{coarse}), foveated vision with fixed gaze, and full-resolution uniform RGB. Coarse separates outright blindness from vision-without-spatial-resolution; foveated occupies a hand-designed middle point on this axis loosely modelled on primate eccentricity-dependent acuity (multi-scale Gaussian blur with quadratic eccentricity falloff; we do \emph{not} model log-polar cortical sampling, see §\ref{sec:methods} pendnote). The architecture is older than current frontier systems, and that is precisely the point: a tractable chassis for a content-level question those systems make pressing but cannot evaluate cleanly.

\noindent\textbf{Contribution.} We propose that cognitive map format in recurrent navigation agents reflects an \emph{emergent capacity allocation} between two routes carrying spatial information --- the visual encoder and the recurrent integration of GPS-sensor history --- with the share each route carries tracking encoder spatial bandwidth. The pattern is consistent with policy gradient routing capacity through one of two paths under finite recurrent memory; we frame this as a hypothesis rather than a directly measured causal mechanism, and discuss the cleanest causal test (within-stack encoder-resolution scaling) as future work in §\ref{sec:limitations}. The principle yields a family of testable predictions about the format, geometry, and population structure of the recurrent state, which we evaluate across the four conditions in §\ref{sec:results}; a preregistered log-polar foveation control adds a 5th intermediate-bandwidth data point on the same axis (Appendix~\ref{app:scaling}). Each prediction draws on a biological precedent (§\ref{sec:bio}), and the four conditions can be read as a controlled silicon analog of sensor-niche-dependent cognitive map formation.

\section{Related Work}

This paper sits at the intersection of four separate lines of work: VLM spatial-reasoning failures whose encoder-vs-data origin remains contested; encoder--memory pipelines in embodied AI rarely characterised at the content level; foveated perception studied for compute and recognition but not memory; and emergent maps in deep-RL navigation, whose dependence on encoder structure remains underexplored.

\noindent\textbf{Spatial-reasoning failures in vision--language models.}\quad Multimodal LLMs with strong surface-level visual recognition systematically fail at fine-grained spatial reasoning~\citep{ramakrishnan2025space,yang2024thinkinginspace,liu2023vsr,kamath2023whatsup,fu2024blink}. Two diagnoses compete. A \emph{data-side} account treats the gap as a training-distribution problem and proposes data-side remedies~\citep{chen2024spatialvlm}. An \emph{encoder-side} account treats the gap as structurally upstream of any downstream training: probing studies show that pretrained encoders trained with different objectives carry markedly different geometric content, with self-supervised models retaining depth and surface structure that vision--language-trained ones largely lack~\citep{tong2024eyeswideshut,elbanani2024probing3d,man2024lexicon3d,cheng2024spatialrgpt,tong2024cambrian}. Our paper sits with the encoder-side account and tests it where the visual sensor is the only varying component --- a controlled setting unavailable at the VLM scale because of architectural and data confounds.

\noindent\textbf{Encoder--memory interface in modern embodied AI.}\quad Recent world models~\citep{hafner2023dreamerv3,zhou2024dinowm,bardes2024vjepa,bruce2024genie} and embodied vision--language--action agents~\citep{driess2023palme,brohan2023rt2,kim2024openvla,wang2023voyager} share an architectural pattern: a pretrained visual encoder feeds an attention- or recurrence-based memory module read by a downstream policy or planner. Practitioner choices already hint the encoder content matters at the level our paper analyses --- some systems fuse multiple pretrained encoders~\citep{kim2024openvla} or build memory on encoders chosen for their geometric content~\citep{zhou2024dinowm}; the frozen-feature-extractor view~\citep{nair2022r3m,majumdar2023vc1} is the limiting case our framing argues against. Whether transformer-based navigators~\citep{zeng2024poliformer} integrate sensor history the way LSTM bottleneck conditions do here is a testable open question.

\noindent\textbf{Foveated and bandwidth-constrained vision.}\quad Peripheral-vision phenomenology~\citep{strasburger2011peripheral,rosenholtz2016peripheral} motivates deep-learning work that applies eccentricity-dependent processing to neural networks, mostly under a compute or recognition prior~\citep{mnih2014attention,sprague2003eyemovements,min2022pervit,shang2023sugarl}. \citet{deza2020foveated} are the closest precedent for treating foveation as content-shaping rather than only compute-saving. \citet{atanov2024photoreceptor} train PointGoalNav agents across a 1-pixel-to-full-camera bandwidth spectrum but do not examine the recurrent state; \citet{pourrahimi2025foveated} probe a foveated visual-search model in a static-scene setting. We extend this line by asking how sensor bandwidth reshapes the format of downstream spatial memory in a recurrent navigation policy.

\noindent\textbf{Emergent maps in deep-RL navigation.}\quad Map-like structure emerges in the recurrent state of navigation agents trained under absent or spatially uniform perception~\citep{wijmans2023emergence,banino2018grid,cueva2018grid,gornet2024cognitive,hennig2023belief,dwivedi2022isee}. Self-supervised path-integration training can produce multi-modular grid cells~\citep{schaeffer2023grid}, though grid-like decoding can be artefactual without careful controls~\citep{schaeffer2023fiction}. This literature studies one condition at a time; we go comparative across a four-condition encoder-bandwidth spectrum, and find what each condition's hidden state encodes about space differs systematically.

\noindent\textbf{Cognitive map theory and place-cell remapping.}\quad The cognitive map hypothesis~\citep{tolman1948cognitive,okeefenadel1978hippocampus} formalises how an agent can navigate flexibly via an internal model of space. Modern computational accounts cast hippocampal codes as predictive maps that compress future-state visit distributions~\citep{stachenfeld2017predictive} and as relational generalisation engines that bind sensory observations to abstract spatial structure~\citep{whittington2020tem,behrens2018cognitive}. Place-cell rate maps remap when the animal's sensory or task context changes~\citep{sanders2020remapping}: rate remapping (firing-rate change at the same locations) versus global remapping (location change altogether) is the central dichotomy used to read whether a representation is bound to or invariant to context. Comparative-cognition work documents how sensor structure shapes navigation strategies across species, from blind subterranean rodents that integrate path information without vision~\citep{kimchi2004molerat}, to bats that build cognitive maps under echolocation~\citep{toledo2020cognitive,geva2015spatial}. We adopt this literature's analysis vocabulary --- Skaggs spatial information~\citep{skaggs1996theta}, rate vs.\ global remapping --- and use the four sensor conditions as silicon analogs of distinct sensory niches, allowing controlled within-architecture variation that animal experiments cannot directly run.

To characterise what each condition's hidden state encodes about space and how that encoding differs across conditions, we draw on four interpretability families that have matured separately: probing classifiers with selectivity controls~\citep{belinkov2022probing,hewitt2019control}; representational geometry~\citep{kornblith2019cka,williams2021shape,facco2017twonn,ansuini2019intrinsic}; causal and behavioural intervention on the recurrent state (memory transplant and shortcut discovery, this work; the principled upgrade is distributed alignment search~\citep{geiger2024finding}); and dynamical-systems analysis~\citep{sussillo2013opening}. Convergence across these families on the same condition-level contrast is the strongest claim our methodology supports.


\section{Methods}

The central claim --- encoder structure shapes what downstream memory encodes about space --- requires a controlled comparison in which the visual sensor varies while task, training, architecture, data, and reward are held identical. The Methods section is organised around the three first-principles requirements this imposes: (i)~a controlled experimental setup (§\ref{sec:methods-setup}); (ii)~a probing protocol that extracts the memory's content without retraining (§\ref{sec:methods-probes}); (iii)~causal and behavioural interventions that test whether probe-readable content is what the policy actually uses (also §\ref{sec:methods-probes}).

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:methods-setup}

All four agents are trained on PointGoal navigation in Habitat~\citep{savva2019habitat} using DD-PPO~\citep{wijmans2020ddppo} on a merged Gibson-0+~\citep{xia2018gibson} (411 scenes) plus Matterport3D-train (61 scenes) pool of 472 training scenes; evaluation uses 18 held-out MP3D test scenes (1008 episodes) for distribution-shift probes. All agents share an identical 3-layer LSTM (512-d) backbone and the Wijmans non-visual sensor stack (goal-in-start-frame, GPS, compass, close-to-goal scalar; each projected to 32-d, concatenated with a 32-d previous-action embedding).

\noindent\textbf{The four conditions.}\quad The conditions sample a two-dimensional design space crossing \emph{encoder spatial output dimension} (none $|$ $1{\times}1$ $|$ $8{\times}8$) with \emph{channel-level information density} (none $|$ collapsed $|$ eccentricity-graded $|$ full):\footnote{\small For brevity we refer to the conditions as a single ``encoder spatial bandwidth axis'' in the abstract and intro; strictly the four conditions are not collinear in this 2-D space. \textcolor{red}{A cleaner single-axis sweep --- varying spatial output dimension while holding channel information constant --- is in progress and will be reported in a follow-up version.}} \emph{blind}, \emph{coarse} (ResNet-18's 32-fold downsampling collapses the feature map to $1{\times}1$, removing spatial structure while preserving channel information), \emph{foveated} (eccentricity-dependent Gaussian blur, $\sigma_{\max}=8$ quadratic falloff, gaze fixed at image centre), and \emph{uniform}. Per-condition input thumbnails, encoder-output dimensions, and roles are visualised in Figure~\ref{fig:setup}. Coarse separates outright blindness from vision-without-spatial-resolution; its $48{\times}48$ input also has lower spatial resolution than uniform's $256{\times}256$, so this single condition cannot fully separate the $1{\times}1$ feature collapse from low input resolution. A log-polar foveation control (Appendix~\ref{app:scaling}) is preregistered to test the encoder-spatial-output mechanism at a single intermediate point ($\sim 2{\times}2$); a partial training run (60M frames) exists but the converged checkpoint is in progress. Gaussian blur is a tractable approximation of foveation; biological foveation uses non-uniform spatial sampling (log-polar) that directly reduces encoder output dimensionality, whereas blur preserves it. The foveated condition disables the running-mean-and-variance input normaliser to avoid an in-place autograd conflict.

\noindent\textbf{Convergence criterion (per-condition).}\quad To avoid ad-hoc per-condition checkpoint selection, we define convergence systematically using three complementary metrics from training-time rollouts: success rate, SPL (Success weighted by Path Length), and final distance-to-goal (DtG). For each cond, we compute a 5M-frame moving average of each metric and identify the smallest training frame $t^*$ at which the smoothed metric is within $\pm 0.02$ (or $\pm 0.5$\,m for DtG) of its trajectory optimum, sustained for at least 30M subsequent frames. The condition's convergence frame is the \emph{maximum} of the three per-metric plateau frames (the strictest criterion the data jointly support). This yields per-cond convergence frames of: blind 342M (success metric does not plateau within our 342M training horizon, matching Wijmans et al.'s slower blind convergence; we report at this horizon), coarse 247M, foveated $> 250$M (SPL plateau not reached by 250M; foveated converges last because path-efficiency continues to improve while success rate plateaus much earlier at 113M), uniform 193M. The probe checkpoints we report (Table~\ref{tab:summary}) are at or beyond these convergence frames for all sighted conditions; blind is reported at the 342M horizon. \uncertain{The foveated 174M canonical seed-0 checkpoint --- forced by the training-stability bug discussed in Appendix~\ref{app:training-stability} --- is below the SPL plateau; we additionally report a fine-tune extension to 250M effective frames (180M pretrained + 70M post-fix; Appendix~\ref{app:training-stability}) and seed-2 at 210M, both confirming the reported pattern.}

To rule out the foveated-under-trained confound at the same time, we additionally report a same-frame 100M anchor (all four conditions at $\sim$100M frames; Appendix~\ref{app:scaling}): the rank ordering Blind $>$ Coarse $>$ Foveated $>$ Uniform is preserved on linear $R^2$ ($0.96 > 0.84 > 0.71 > 0.23$) and on LOSO median $R^2$ ($0.92 > 0.74 > 0.34 > -0.60$), confirming the bandwidth--allocation tradeoff is not an artefact of differing training durations.

\noindent\textbf{Frame budgets and seeds.}\quad Frame budgets reflect per-condition convergence as defined above. All four reach near-equivalent task competence (success $\in [0.93, 0.99]$, §\ref{sec:summary}). \textcolor{red}{All main-text magnitudes use a single seed per condition.}\footnote{\small \textcolor{red}{Two-seed replication for foveated and uniform has landed: foveated GPS $R^2$ = $+0.06$ (seed-0, 174M) vs $+0.10$ (seed-2, 210M); uniform GPS $R^2$ = $-0.31$ (seed-0, 250M) vs $-1.54$ (seed-2, 225M). Both pairs preserve rank-ordering (rich-encoder regime) and \emph{sign}; magnitudes drift within the single-seed standard deviations already reported. Multi-seed replication for blind and coarse is in progress.}}

\subsection{Probing and intervention protocols}
\label{sec:methods-probes}

After training, all weights are frozen. We collect per-step top-layer LSTM hidden states ($\mathbf{h}_2$, 512-d) paired with ground-truth labels from $n=500$ Gibson held-out episodes under \emph{deterministic} (argmax-action) rollouts.\footnote{\small We focus on $\mathbf{h}_2$ because the linear policy head reads it; full Ridge results across all (hidden, cell) $\times$ (L0, L1, L2) states are in Appendix~\ref{fig:supp_extra_states}. The alternative stochastic-action protocol gives degenerate $\sim 4$-step rollouts (Appendix~\ref{app:probing-details}); all probing in this paper uses deterministic rollouts to match our shortcut and transplant protocols.} The same hidden states feed three method families that supply independent angles on the same condition-level contrast.

\noindent\emph{Decodability.}\quad Ridge linear probes ($\alpha=10$) are fit with episode-level 5-fold cross-validation; we report mean $\pm \sigma$ across folds and verify probe specificity via Hewitt--Liang label-permutation selectivity~\citep{hewitt2019control}. Probed targets: GPS, compass, distance-to-goal (§\ref{sec:h1}); cross-condition transfer (train probe on condition $A$, test on $B$) for H2 (§\ref{sec:h2}); visited-region occupancy, metric $20{\times}20$ occupancy grid, ego-frame goal vector (§\ref{sec:boundaries}). All decodability claims here are about \emph{linear} decodability --- the property the policy's linear head actually consumes; non-linear codes recoverable by a 2-layer MLP but not by linear probes are reported separately in Appendix~\ref{app:probing-details}, and the relevant H2 claim is therefore that subspaces are \emph{linearly} disjoint, not orthogonal in any stronger sense. The cross-condition transfer null hypothesis is that under identical formats, transfer $R^2$ approaches self-transfer $R^2$; our finding (negative or near-chance transfer) bounds the format-shared component well below this null.

\noindent\emph{Representational geometry.}\quad Linear CKA~\citep{kornblith2019cka} for cross-condition similarity, 1-NN purity on pooled hidden states, generalised Procrustes shape metric~\citep{williams2021shape} (a true metric satisfying the triangle inequality, addressing known CKA failure modes), and participation ratio plus TwoNN intrinsic dimension~\citep{facco2017twonn,ansuini2019intrinsic} as architecture-agnostic complexity scalars.

\noindent\emph{Causal and behavioural intervention.}\quad \emph{Memory transplant}: paste a donor's hidden state into the recipient at step 30, compare baseline / self-transplant / cross-transplant SPL across 150 episodes per pair --- the cross-vs-self gap isolates condition-mismatch from rollout-divergence noise (midpoint sweep in Figure~\ref{fig:transplant_sweep_supp}; the cross-self gap peaks in $t \in [30, 90]$ where the agent has integrated history but is not yet committed). \emph{Shortcut discovery}: paired same-scene-different-goal episodes (20 scenes $\times$ 10 pairs); compare second-episode SPL under reset vs.\ persistent LSTM; the SPL drop indexes how tightly the policy couples actions to integrated recurrent memory (§\ref{sec:dissociation}). \emph{Mid-rollout sensor ablation}: zero GPS and compass from step 0 (§\ref{sec:dissociation}). All zero-input ablations push the LSTM off its training input distribution, so probe-level statistics on the resulting trajectory cannot be interpreted at face value; we therefore use the GPS-zero ablation only at the policy-output (success) level, where condition-wise success collapse is interpretable behaviourally even though the underlying $\mathbf{h}$ trajectory is OOD. The complementary visual-zero ablation was attempted for the substitution mechanism (§\ref{sec:h1}) but is contaminated by off-manifold LSTM dynamics under zeroed visual input; we therefore do not include it as causal evidence. \textcolor{red}{An in-distribution alternative --- replacing GPS/compass at step 0 with task-irrelevant signals matched to the training-time input distribution rather than zero --- is the cleaner causal intervention.}\footnote{\small \textcolor{red}{In-distribution sensor ablation requires either training a separate intervention model or sampling marginals from training trajectories at step 0; we are running this as a planned follow-up to provide causal evidence less vulnerable to OOD contamination.}}

For brevity below, families 1 and 2 are referred to as ``probes'' and ``geometry''; family 3 as ``intervention''. Convergence across all three on the same condition-level contrast is the strongest claim our methodology supports.

\noindent\textbf{Scope.}\quad The protocol is calibrated to detect (i)~linear-readability differences in $\mathbf{h}_2$, (ii)~cross-condition geometric divergence in the same coordinates the policy reads, and (iii)~policy-level reliance on integrated recurrent memory. It is \emph{not} calibrated to (a)~separate non-linear from linear codes (the appendix MLP-probe results bound but do not resolve this), (b)~establish causal mechanism beyond correlation between probe-readability and behavioural reliance, or (c)~estimate per-condition variance over training seeds. \textcolor{red}{Items (a) and (c) are addressed in the in-progress follow-ups noted above; (b) requires the dynamical-systems / fixed-point analysis that this paper flags as the next step (§\ref{sec:limitations}).}\footnote{\small \textcolor{red}{Specifically: a 2-layer MLP probe sweep in the main text (currently appendix-only), multi-seed replication, and Sussillo--Barak fixed-point analysis on the H2 contrast are the three highest-priority follow-up methods experiments.}}


\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Comparable navigation skill across all four conditions}
\label{sec:summary}

Table~\ref{tab:summary} collects the headline per-condition numbers. \emph{All four agents solve the same task at near-equivalent competence.} Sighted conditions reach $96$--$99\%$ success; blind reaches $93\%$ at a longer training budget, reproducing Wijmans et al.~\citep{wijmans2023emergence}. The narrow success bracket rules out a skill-gap account for any encoded-content differences below. The probe machinery is calibrated: distance-to-goal decodes everywhere ($R^2 \in [0.81, 0.90]$), demonstrating Ridge has the capacity to fit high-$R^2$ scalars when the hidden state contains them; Hewitt--Liang label-permutation selectivity is high precisely where GPS is high (Appendix~\ref{app:probing-details}).A classical Bug-style controller (greedy goal-seeker with always-right wall-recovery, same GPS+compass sensor stack, no learned weights) achieves SPL $0.07$ / success $0.13$ on the same Gibson eval scenes --- an order of magnitude below our agents' $0.59$--$0.85$ SPL range, supporting that the agents are doing real navigation rather than coincidental wall-following. The behavioural near-equivalence sets up the question the rest of §\ref{sec:results} addresses: \emph{what differs internally}, given that what the agents \emph{do} is the same?

\noindent\textbf{Information allocation: total info shifts gradually, linear-readable info shifts much faster}\quad Capacity allocation between encoder and memory routes makes a graded prediction about total position information in $\mathbf{h}_2$: it should decrease with encoder bandwidth (some pos-info shifts from $\mathbf{h}_2$ to encoder route), but the linear-readable component should decrease much faster (rich-encoder agents push the residual position code into non-linear $\mathbf{h}_2$ directions). Three measurements give this prediction quantitative shape (Figure~\ref{fig:information_allocation}, Appendix~\ref{app:mine}). Linear (Ridge $\alpha = 10$) GPS $R^2$ varies dramatically across conditions ($+0.95$ blind, $+0.72$ coarse, $+0.25$ foveated$^*$, $-1.08$ uniform; range $\approx 2.0$). A 2-layer MLP probe (hidden $=256$, $L_2 = 10^{-4}$, same 5-fold episode-level CV) compresses the range substantially ($+0.95$ blind, $+0.81$ coarse, $+0.62$ foveated$^*$, $+0.48$ uniform; range $\approx 0.5$). Direct mutual-information estimation via MINE~\citep{belghazi2018mine} (Appendix~\ref{app:mine}) gives a nat-scale anchor: $I(\mathbf{h}_2; \text{pos})$ decreases monotonically and gradually from $4.6$ bits (blind) $\to 3.8$ (coarse) $\to 3.4$ (foveated$^*$) $\to 2.9$ (uniform), a $\approx 1.5\times$ ratio across conditions. The gap between MLP and linear $R^2$ widens with encoder bandwidth: $\sim 0$ for blind (information is purely linear), $+0.10$ for coarse, $+0.37$ for foveated$^*$, $+1.56$ for uniform. This is the precise form capacity allocation takes: \emph{total recoverable position information shifts gradually with encoder bandwidth ($\approx 1.5\times$ from blind to uniform), while linear-readable information shifts much faster (effectively factor-of-zero for uniform). Bottleneck conditions concentrate position into a linear top-layer direction; rich-encoder conditions push the residual position-code into non-linear $\mathbf{h}_2$ directions, while also offloading some position information to the visual encoder route.} \uncertain{$^*$All foveated values in this paragraph are at the seed-0 $174$M canonical checkpoint (below SPL plateau per §\ref{sec:methods}); a pretrained-init fine-tune to $250$M effective is in progress and will tighten these magnitudes.}

\noindent\textbf{Temporal stability is the precise claim}\quad Step-binned probing (Figure~\ref{fig:h1_mega}b) separates ``never had a top-layer GPS code'' from ``had one mid-episode then lost linear readability''; rich-encoder conditions are the latter. The Table~\ref{tab:summary} ``rich-encoder $\approx$ chance'' headline is therefore long-tail behaviour, not mid-episode absence. \emph{H1 is most precisely a temporal-stability claim about the linearly-decodable top-layer GPS code.} A lag-$k$ probe corroborates: blind sustains GPS at $k=20$, while rich-encoder conditions are at chance for every $k \leq 10$.

\emph{Pipeline view: where GPS sits, and where it disappears.} Tracing GPS $R^2$ from the encoder feature-map up to the policy-readable top layer (Figure~\ref{fig:h1_mega}c) localises the H1 divergence. The encoder is at chance for all sighted conditions, so H1 is not an encoder-level retention property --- encoders are roughly equally GPS-blind. The L0 jump is the GPS-sensor embedding entering the LSTM input vector, not the network computing position. The divergence is specifically at L2, the policy readout: bottleneck conditions hold their L0 GPS through L2, rich-encoder conditions discard it. Since the DD-PPO action head is a linear projection of $\mathbf{h}_2$, ``linearly decodable at L2'' maps directly to ``policy-accessible''. A 2-layer MLP on the same L2 states does recover GPS for rich-encoder conditions, so H1 is precisely about \emph{linear} top-layer readability, not absence of GPS information.\pendnote{The single-seed cross-dataset shift on MP3D --- foveated GPS at chance $\to +0.35$ --- awaits multi-seed replication; if it survives, it would suggest a condition-specific MP3D recovery worth investigating, but at $N=1$ we cannot rule out per-seed noise. The MLP result alone cannot exclude exploitation of position-correlated features; Appendix~\ref{app:probe-details} discusses both.} The blind result replicates Wijmans et al.~\citep{wijmans2023emergence}; coarse extends their finding to a condition with visual input but a $1{\times}1$ encoder output --- the shared trigger is minimal visual-feature variety per step, not absence of vision.

\noindent\textbf{Capacity-reallocation mechanism, with cross-training evidence}\quad The MLP recovery shows GPS information is present at L2 in rich-encoder conditions; the question is why the LSTM stops carrying it linearly. We propose H1 is a \emph{capacity-reallocation} effect: integrating the L0 GPS-sensor input across time and exploiting the encoder's position-correlated visual features are two routes competing for finite hidden-state capacity. Bottleneck conditions force capacity to the integration route (no rich visual features available), and the temporally-stable GPS code persists in $\mathbf{h}_2$. Rich-encoder conditions provide a viable visual route; policy gradient reallocates capacity to it, and the integrated code is no longer linearly readable from the top layer --- not absent, just spent elsewhere.

Cross-training probes at $4$--$5$ training checkpoints per condition (Figure~\ref{fig:substitution_dynamics}) support this account. Both rich-encoder conditions (foveated, uniform) show a clear early-positive GPS $R^2$ at $\sim 50$M frames ($R^2 \in [0.67, 0.79]$, tight 5-fold spread $\sigma \leq 0.18$ comparable to bottleneck early-training spread), followed by a regime where the same probe gives no longer reliably positive $R^2$ with much wider spread (consistent with probe extrapolation failure when no linear signal is present). Bottleneck conditions stay tight and high throughout. \uncertain{The two rich-encoder conditions also differ in \emph{decay rate}: uniform's GPS $R^2$ has already left tight-positive by $\sim 100$M frames, foveated still has $R^2 = +0.78$ at $\sim 100$M frames before crashing by $\sim 150$M. We read this as the substitution timescale tracking encoder informativeness about position --- uniform substitutes fastest, foveated slower because peripheral acuity is degraded.} The early-then-decay contrast is both within-episode (Figure~\ref{fig:h1_mega}b) and across training: rich-encoder agents learn an integration code transiently, then unlearn its linear top-layer readability as the visual route consolidates. Compass codes follow the same bottleneck-vs-rich-encoder pattern (Figure~\ref{fig:substitution_dynamics}b), broadening the substitution claim from GPS specifically to spatial codes generally; uniform's compass partially survives ($R^2 \approx +0.36$ at convergence) where its GPS does not, suggesting heading is the more recoverable component of the visual-route substitute.\pendnote{Per-checkpoint probes are single-seed; multi-seed replication is left for future work. The decay-rate ordering is most exposed to seed variability. A behavioural causal probe (mid-rollout GPS-sensor ablation) is reported in §\ref{sec:dissociation}; a representational causal probe (mid-rollout encoder-output zeroing, which severs the visual route in-manifold) is left for future work.}

\subsection{Scene-invariance is the precise mechanism: why bottlenecks decode linearly and rich encoders do not (H3)}
\label{sec:h3-scene-invariance}

The linear$\to$MLP gap (Figure~\ref{fig:information_allocation}) shows position information is approximately preserved by MLP probing across conditions even when linear readability collapses --- a format shift rather than a total-information collapse (MINE shows a more modest $\approx 1.5\times$ decrease in total $I(\mathbf{h}_2; \text{pos})$; Appendix~\ref{app:mine}). What changes \emph{format} between bottleneck and rich-encoder conditions? A leave-one-scene-out (LOSO) cross-validation localises the answer: for each held-out Gibson scene, fit Ridge on the other $\sim 30$ scenes and report test $R^2$ on the held-out scene (Figure~\ref{fig:loso_cv}). Blind achieves median LOSO $R^2 = 0.92$ with $0\%$ of held-out scenes giving negative $R^2$; coarse drops to median $0.17$ ($48\%$ scenes negative); foveated$^*$ $0.28$ ($38\%$); uniform $0.15$ ($44\%$). \uncertain{$^*$Foveated value is at $174$M (below SPL plateau, see §\ref{sec:methods}); will be re-measured at the $250$M-effective fine-tune in progress.} The same rank ordering holds at the 100M same-frame anchor (Appendix~\ref{app:scaling}), confirming the bottleneck-vs-rich-encoder pattern is not an artefact of differing training durations within early-training; the $250$M-effective re-probe will further test whether the ordering holds at strict per-cond convergence.

The interpretation: encoder bandwidth modulates not the \emph{availability} of position information in $\mathbf{h}_2$ (preserved, per the MLP probe) but the \emph{scene-invariance} of the linear readout direction. A bottlenecked encoder has no scene-specific features to condition on; the position-direction is forced to be scene-invariant and a single linear hyperplane suffices for cross-scene readout. A rich encoder lets the LSTM use scene-specific visual features to encode position; different scenes induce different position-directions in $\mathbf{h}_2$, and a single linear probe cannot capture this scene-conditional encoding while an MLP can by switching its readout on visual context. This re-states capacity allocation in IB-invariance terms~\citep{achille2018invariance}: bottleneck-induced encoding is invariance-disentangled, rich-encoder encoding is scene-conditional disentanglement.

\label{sec:h2}

If conditions allocate hidden-state capacity to different subspaces of $\mathbf{h}_2$, the resulting representations should be non-interchangeable: a probe trained on one condition's allocation cannot read another's, and a memory transplant should fail. Two complementary tests confirm this.

\noindent\textbf{Behavioural test (memory transplant)}\quad Pasting a donor's mid-episode hidden state into a recipient's policy at step $30$ tests whether the representational geometry differences \emph{matter} outside the probe framework (Figure~\ref{fig:h2}, right). Three structural facts emerge that no linear-similarity measure on its own would surface. \emph{Asymmetry along the bottleneck axis:} bottleneck-donor states are toxic to rich-encoder recipients while the reverse direction is benign --- the rich-encoder LSTM cannot make sense of bottleneck-donor activity at the policy readout, but a bottleneck LSTM can still drive its policy from rich-encoder donor activity. \emph{Recipient brittleness scales with encoder bandwidth:} uniform suffers the largest cost from \emph{any} foreign donor; blind tolerates them all. \emph{The linear-similarity blind spot:} pairs that read as indistinguishable under unaligned linear CKA differ by $0.01$--$0.38$ SPL under transplant --- ``these representations look identical to a linear similarity metric'' is consistent with ``their actual policy-relevant content is incompatible''.\pendnote{Single-seed; the asymmetry pattern is qualitatively robust across the full $4{\times}4$ matrix but specific cell magnitudes will move with replication.}

\emph{Convergent evidence at the probe level.} Cross-condition probe transfer (Figure~\ref{fig:h2}, left) shows the same asymmetric pattern: probes trained on bottleneck states predict off-manifold by a wider margin on rich-encoder hidden states than the reverse. The 1-NN purity is $1.000$ vs.\ chance $0.25$ on $1500{\times}4$ pooled top-layer states --- a sample's nearest neighbour by Euclidean distance is always within its own condition. The same $1.000$ holds at $10\,000{\times}4$ pooled samples (a $6.7{\times}$ enlargement), confirming the result is not a sample-size artefact. Together with the symmetrically catastrophic transfer failure (every off-diagonal cell), this rules out both the ``one code embedded in another'' alternative and the ``same code at different scale'' alternative --- scaled embeddings would still admit cross-probe transfer.\footnote{\small Methodological reassurance: probe capacity is not the bottleneck (self-probes succeed everywhere); sample-size asymmetry is ruled out by common-sample truncation; task competence is bunched ($96$--$99\%$, §\ref{sec:summary}), so the divergence is not a skill difference. Boundaries: similarity tests are linear or near-linear, so non-linear measures could reveal higher-order shared structure; the four sensors are diverse but not exhaustive.}

\noindent\textbf{Geometric evidence: shape-metric distances and intrinsic-dimension scalars}\quad Linear CKA tells us conditions look ``not similar'' but provides neither a true metric nor a complexity scalar; we add two methodologically distinct geometric views (Table~\ref{tab:geometry}). \emph{Shape-metric distance matrix}~\citep{williams2021shape}: the Procrustes size-and-shape distance $d_1$ and the Riemannian angular distance $\theta_1$ on Kendall's shape space (computed on $500$ paired episode-mean $\mathbf{h}_2$ vectors per condition, optimal-rotation-aligned in closed form). Both $d_1$ and $\theta_1$ are \emph{true metrics}~--- they satisfy the triangle inequality and embed in Euclidean space, two properties linear CKA cannot guarantee~--- and they pull \emph{coarse} out as the most-distant point from every other condition (mean $d_1 \approx 8.8$k vs.\ $\approx 4.0$k among the rest); under the angular metric, blind sits closest to coarse ($\theta_1 = 1.10$, the bottleneck pair) while uniform and foveated form a tighter rich-encoder pair ($\theta_1 = 1.08$). \emph{Within-condition complexity scalars}~\citep{ansuini2019intrinsic}: participation ratio (PR) and TwoNN intrinsic dimension. \noindent\textbf{Direct subspace test: principal angles and position-direction alignment}\quad The capacity-allocation principle predicts that conditions occupy mutually orthogonal subspaces of $\mathbf{h}_2$, with position encoded along non-aligned directions. We test this directly (Figure~\ref{fig:subspace_divergence}). For each condition, we extract the top-$K$ PCA basis of $\mathbf{h}_2$ ($K \approx 8$--$10$, covering $\sim 90\%$ variance) and the position-encoding direction (Ridge probe weight $\boldsymbol\beta$ for GPS). Across all six condition pairs, mean principal angles between top-$K$ subspaces lie in $[86°, 89°]$ --- maximally close to orthogonal in this hidden-state geometry --- and pairwise cosines between position-encoding directions lie in $[-0.04, +0.02]$, indistinguishable from random. Conditions allocate to mutually orthogonal subspaces, and within those subspaces position is encoded along non-aligned directions. This is the subspace-divergence prediction in its sharpest form.\pendnote{Single seed; multi-seed replication is left for future work. Random pairs in 512-d would have expected principal angles slightly below 90° due to finite $K$; our values match this null up to 1--4°, indicating the subspaces are statistically indistinguishable from random orientations.}

\noindent\textbf{Capacity allocation is established early, refined late}\quad The convergence-time orthogonality in Figure~\ref{fig:subspace_divergence} could in principle reflect either (i) a late-emerging differentiation that builds slowly through training or (ii) a structural commitment laid down early and refined toward maximal orthogonality. The capacity allocation that produces non-interchangeable subspaces is therefore a training-time process whose direction is committed early; the convergence-time orthogonality measured in Figure~\ref{fig:subspace_divergence} is a refinement of structure already present at $\sim 50$M frames, not a late-emerging novelty.\pendnote{Single seed; multi-seed replication is left for future work. \subsection{Foveation as hybrid allocation: shared magnitude with rich-encoder, distinct geometry}
\label{sec:foveation}

Foveation's allocation is hybrid: it groups with uniform on the bandwidth--allocation tradeoff (rich-encoder magnitude, §\ref{sec:h1}), but separates from uniform on every other measure (Table~\ref{tab:fov-vs-uni}) --- consistent with the capacity-allocation principle that the visual and integration routes are independent sinks rather than two ends of one knob, so foveation can spend capacity differently from uniform within the rich-encoder regime.

\subsection{Availability vs.\ consumption: probe-readable capacity does not predict policy reliance}
\label{sec:dissociation}

Probe-readable position indexes \emph{where capacity is allocated}; the shortcut-discovery experiment indexes \emph{which route the policy consumes}. Under the capacity-allocation principle, these are independent quantities and need not coincide. Paired same-scene-different-goal episodes ($20$ scenes $\times$ $10$ pairs) compare second-episode SPL under reset vs.\ persistent LSTM; the SPL drop indexes how tightly the policy couples actions to integrated recurrent memory. Plotted against probe-readable top-layer GPS, two off-diagonal cases emerge. \emph{Coarse} carries a linear top-layer GPS code yet has a low shortcut SPL drop --- its policy under-weights the readable GPS. \emph{Uniform} has no linear top-layer GPS yet has a high shortcut SPL drop; \uncertain{the policy must therefore be reading a non-spatial substitute --- plausibly scene-history features the linear-GPS probe is blind to.} ``Probe-readable'' and ``policy-relied-on'' can therefore dissociate in either direction (visualised as the marker-size axis of Figure~\ref{fig:synthesis_2axes}, §\ref{sec:discussion}). We label these as candidate dissociations rather than established anomalies.\pendnote{The 5 points are single-seed; confirming the dissociation (rather than off-diagonal points sitting within seed-to-seed noise) requires multi-seed replication left for future work.}

\noindent\textbf{Memory-init transplant test.}\quad The §\ref{sec:h2} memory-transplant experiment swaps a donor's hidden state into a recipient mid-episode. Here we test the converse: re-initialise the \emph{same} agent at episode start with its own previous-episode-end memory $(\mathbf{h}_T, \mathbf{c}_T)$, and ask whether navigating S$\to$T is helped or hurt by carrying scene-context from the trajectory just completed. This isolates whether the trained policy's memory is \emph{scene-generic} (would generalise across runs of the same task) or \emph{trajectory-bound} (only meaningful in the integration context where it was generated). All four conditions show \emph{consistent negative} $\Delta$SPL (range $-0.10$ to $-0.14$ across conditions; probe initialised with own final memory \emph{vs.\ }zero-init agent baseline; single seed, $n{=}150$ episodes/condition). The robust direction across H1-divergent conditions says \emph{the policy at $t{=}0$ expects a zero hidden state}; non-zero initialisation hurts navigation regardless of encoder type. This complements the subspace-divergence finding (§\ref{sec:h2}): the capacity allocation is not just \emph{cross-condition} non-interchangeable, it is \emph{within-condition} trajectory-bound — the trained policy's mapping from memory to action is calibrated to the specific integration trajectory the LSTM rolled out, not to a scene-generic representation. Blind shows the largest absolute drop (success $0.81 \to 0.67$); the other three retain $\geq 90$\% success but lose path-efficiency.\pendnote{Single seed; multi-seed replication is left for future work.}

If uniform's memory is non-spatial, what does it anchor to? Trajectory-level analysis of the persistent-memory failures gives a candidate answer (Figure~\ref{fig:shortcut_paired_traj}). Across same-floor paired-episode failures, only uniform has a meaningful sample whose persistent agent ends up closer to the \emph{previous} episode's goal than the new one (margin $+1.83$m on $n{=}46$); blind, coarse and foveated all end nearer the new goal but fail to reach it.\pendnote{Single-seed; multi-seed replication is left for future work.} \uncertain{This suggests uniform's persistent failure mechanism is a visual / scene-history anchor: the LSTM tells the policy ``head to where you were going last episode''. Blind's small negative margin ($-0.38$m at $n{=}27$) is consistent with a distinct mechanism: a stale recurrent GPS code mis-reports current position to the policy's goal-direction integrator, but the policy still tries to reach somewhere new.}

\noindent\textbf{Behavioural validation under GPS-sensor ablation.}\quad A complementary behavioural test asks whether the policy reads the GPS sensor specifically (and which conditions tolerate its loss). We zero the GPS and compass inputs from step $0$ in each rollout (the L0 sensor signals that drive the integration route), then measure end-of-episode success rate ($n{=}50$--$100$ episodes/cond, single seed). All four conditions collapse to $3$--$8\%$ success (vs.\ baseline $93$--$99\%$), indicating that none of them navigates from purely visual landmarks --- the GPS sensor is critical across the spectrum. The size of the drop modestly tracks the dissociation direction: \emph{coarse} (highest probe-readable GPS, $R^2 = 0.78$) drops the most ($\Delta = -0.95$, success $0.98 \to 0.03$), and \emph{uniform} (no probe-readable GPS, policy reading non-spatial substitutes) drops the least ($\Delta = -0.91$, success $0.99 \to 0.08$); foveated and blind fall between ($\Delta = -0.92$ and $-0.87$ respectively). The $4$--$8\,\text{pp}$ inter-condition spread is small relative to the absolute drop and within plausible single-seed noise; we report this as consistent-but-not-conclusive support for the §\ref{sec:dissociation} dissociation rather than independent confirmation.\pendnote{Single seed; multi-seed replication is left for future work. The mid-rollout zeroed visual ablation (a stronger causal test of the bandwidth--allocation tradeoff in §\ref{sec:h1}) was contaminated by the LSTM going off its training manifold under unseen zero-input distribution; we report only the behavioural success rate from GPS ablation, which is a policy-level signal independent of probe extrapolation.}

\subsection{Boundaries: null results that bracket the capacity-allocation principle}
\label{sec:boundaries}

What does \emph{not} discriminate also defines what our H1/H2 claims are about. Three null results bracket our findings. \emph{(i) Low-resolution (visited-region) occupancy} decodes above chance everywhere --- something coarse-grained about where the agent has been is in every condition's hidden state, regardless of sensor structure. \emph{(ii) Metric $20{\times}20$ occupancy} fails everywhere (consistent with~\citep{ramakrishnan2025space}: high-resolution metric layout needs non-linear probes). \emph{(iii) The ego-frame goal vector} is at chance everywhere, because PointGoal supplies it as a per-step input — the policy has no incentive to re-encode it. Together these say H1 / H2 are specifically about the \emph{linearly-decodable, world-frame position code at the policy readout} and not about every spatial signal that might live in the recurrent state.

\noindent\textbf{Allocentric occupancy decoder.}\quad \hcpending{To test whether each condition's final memory $(\mathbf{h}_T, \mathbf{c}_T)$ encodes a metric \emph{map} of traversed free-space (not just the linear position scalar), we train a small per-condition MLP decoder $f(\mathbf{h}_T, \mathbf{c}_T) \to$ 32$\times$32 binary occupancy grid (16m$\times$16m at 0.5m/cell, centred on episode start; loss restricted to cells within 2.5m of the trajectory; 5-fold episode-level CV). \uncertain{Expected per-condition pattern: Blind / Coarse decode metric maps from the integrated GPS-sensor history; Uniform / Foveated should decode less if memory is not map-encoding. If Uniform / Foveated IoU is comparably high, the H1 ``rich-encoder discards spatial code'' framing needs refinement: the spatial map exists but is not linearly readable as a position scalar; if low, the H1 mechanism story is corroborated. Decoder is left for future work.}}

A counter-intuitive boundary observation comes from population coding (Appendix~\ref{app:supp_figs}): rich-encoder conditions have a few units with \emph{higher} peak spatial information than blind, yet their hidden states do not linearly decode GPS. \uncertain{We interpret this as rich-encoder peaked units encoding features correlated with position (visual landmarks, trajectory phase) rather than position itself; blind's broader, lower-peak distribution is what carries the linearly-decodable position code.} This is the inverse of the naive expectation that more peaked spatial tuning $\to$ better position decoding, and sharpens what H1 measures: \emph{linear, top-layer, policy-readable} position, not per-unit spatial information.

\noindent\textbf{Excursion-forgetting separates blind from the integrated-code regime.}\quad A complementary perturbation test distinguishes \emph{blind's} robustness from the \emph{integrated-code} regime that coarse and the rich-encoder conditions share. We force a 25-step random-action detour mid-episode ($50$-step warmup $\to$ $25$-step detour $\to$ $100$-step recovery; $n=100$ episodes/condition), train one Ridge probe on full-episode pooled hidden states (episode-level 5-fold CV), and measure scale-invariant prediction error (MAE / position-spread) per segment. All four conditions show \emph{forgetting} (recovery error $>$ warmup error), but with markedly different magnitudes: \uncertain{blind $+0.17$, uniform $+0.31$, foveated $+0.34$, coarse $+0.43$ (Figure~\ref{fig:wjf_excursion}).} Blind's forgetting is roughly half that of the other three, consistent with its hidden state being closer to a per-step ``GPS-sensor passthrough'' (the sensor still reads correctly after the detour) than to an integrated path-integration code. The other three (including coarse, the other bottleneck condition) cluster around a more fragile integrated regime: coarse integrates GPS over time, rich-encoder agents commit some role to visual context — both decohere when the action stream is interrupted.\pendnote{Single-seed; multi-seed replication left for future work. Variance-matched MAE/spread metric corrects a prior R$^2$-based artefact where target-spread differences across segments inflated apparent recovery; full methodology in `excursion\_analyze\_v2.py`.}

\subsection{Synthesis: five views of one capacity allocation}

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{fig/fig6_synthesis_2axes.pdf}
\caption{Synthesis: three orthogonal measurements on the same four conditions, chosen for the synthesis figure to summarise the high-level story. \emph{X-axis:} top-layer GPS $R^2$ on $\mathbf{h}_2$ (H1 magnitude). \emph{Y-axis:} $-1\times$ average cross-condition transplant cost when this condition is the donor (H2 format isolation; higher Y = more disruptive to other conditions' policies). \emph{Marker size:} second-episode shortcut SPL drop $\%$ (larger = more policy dependence on persistent recurrent memory). The information-allocation, scene-invariance, and place-cell findings (Figures~\ref{fig:information_allocation}, \ref{fig:loso_cv}, \ref{fig:place_cells}) sharpen the \emph{interpretation} of each axis but are not separate axes of variation.}
\label{fig:synthesis_2axes}
\end{figure}

The five predictions cohere as views of one capacity allocation (Figure~\ref{fig:synthesis_2axes}, summarised on three axes for visual compactness). The \emph{magnitude axis (H1, X)} is how much capacity the integration route receives: bottleneck conditions retain a linear top-layer GPS code, rich-encoder ones reallocate to the visual route (§\ref{sec:h1}). The information-allocation, scene-invariance, predictive-horizon, and place-cell findings sharpen what this axis \emph{means}: total recoverable position information shifts gradually with encoder bandwidth ($\approx 1.5\times$ from blind to uniform per MINE; Appendix~\ref{app:mine}); what differs much faster is whether the position-axis is scene-invariant and linearly accessible (bottleneck) or scene-conditional and only nonlinearly accessible (rich encoder, Figure~\ref{fig:loso_cv}); whether $\mathbf{h}_t$ encodes future positions linearly (bottleneck: yes, up to $k = 20$ steps; rich-encoder: no; Appendix~\ref{app:predictive_horizon}); and at the population level, per-unit Skaggs spatial information is similar across conditions but place-unit \emph{count} and place-vs-view-cell character differ (Figure~\ref{fig:place_cells}). The \emph{format axis (H2, Y)} captures which subspace the allocation lands in: cross-condition memory transplants are asymmetric, per-condition probes are mutually off-manifold, and probe-transfer $R^2$ is catastrophically negative across conditions --- subspaces are not just orthogonal but jointly incompatible at the readout level (§\ref{sec:h2}, Appendix~\ref{app:cap_allocation_extras}). The \emph{consumption axis (marker size)} is the §\ref{sec:dissociation} shortcut SPL drop: which route the policy actually reads. Each condition occupies a distinct (X, Y, size) coordinate, and the bandwidth--allocation tradeoff (H1) is one of three orthogonal axes, not the whole picture.

\uncertain{The capacity-allocation principle plays out differently in coarse and uniform even though both lie on the magnitude axis: coarse's encoder cannot supply spatially-resolved features, so capacity is allocated to integrating the GPS-sensor signal; uniform's encoder supplies rich features and capacity is reallocated to the visual route, leaving the integrated GPS code unattended in $\mathbf{h}_2$. The two are not the same mechanism, only two regimes of one allocation.}
\subsection{Biological precedent: sensor structure shapes hippocampal representation}
\label{sec:bio}

The principle that sensor structure constrains spatial-memory format is not one we propose; biological evidence for it predates ours by decades. Our contribution is to isolate the principle in a controlled silicon model where the encoder, memory, policy, training distribution and reward are held fixed and only the visual sensor varies. Two threads of animal-navigation research are particularly close to the H1 and H2 findings; the dissociation finding (probe-readable vs.\ policy-used memory) does not have an established direct biological analog but echoes a broader theme that ``having a representation'' and ``using it for behaviour'' are dissociable in animal cognition more generally.

\emph{H1 (sensor bottleneck $\to$ memory takes over).} Congenitally blind humans show enhanced occipital--hippocampal coupling during spatial tasks~\citep{kupers2014blind}, consistent with hippocampus carrying more navigational load when the visual stream is absent. Rodent grid-cell periodicity collapses under prolonged darkness while the animal still navigates~\citep{chen2016grid}, indicating spatial behaviour can be sustained when the visual landmark stream is removed. Both are biological analogs of our blind / coarse regime, where the LSTM --- deprived of rich visual variety per step --- retains a linear top-layer GPS code that is no longer linearly readable in the rich-encoder conditions.

\emph{H2 (different sensors $\to$ different spatial codes).} The primate vs.\ rodent contrast in hippocampal cell types --- view-cells dominant in primates, place-cells dominant in rodents --- has been attributed in part to the foveated-with-saccades vs.\ near-panoramic visual sampling regime each species uses~\citep{rolls2024why}. Cross-modality remapping in echolocating bats, where the same neurons recode under vision vs.\ echolocation~\citep{geva2016remapping}, demonstrates that sensor modality alters the linear subspace of hippocampal coding. Both parallel our H2 finding: the same task with different visual structure yields linearly separable LSTM subspaces (CKA $<10^{-4}$, probe-transfer collapse, asymmetric memory transplant).

We treat these as convergent (not conclusive) evidence. Convergence across systems --- and across silicon vs.\ neural --- is suggestive of a sensor--memory coupling principle more general than any single architecture, but it does not show that the mechanisms are the same. Our experiments isolate the principle in a controlled, single-architecture setting; biological work establishes that the principle exists in animals at all. The two are complementary; neither is decisive alone.

\noindent\textbf{Spatial information per LSTM unit (canonical Skaggs).}\quad To anchor the biological precedent quantitatively, we apply the canonical Skaggs spatial-information formula~\citep{skaggs1996theta},
\[
I = \sum_b p(b) \, \frac{\lambda_b}{\bar\lambda} \, \log_2 \frac{\lambda_b}{\bar\lambda} \quad [\text{bits/sample}],
\]
to each LSTM unit, using rectified activation $\max(h, 0)$ as the proxy ``firing rate'' $\lambda_b$ in spatial bin $b$ (Figure~\ref{fig:place_cells}). The pattern across conditions is, with this canonical metric, surprisingly \emph{flat}: mean Skaggs bits per (unit, scene) is $1.25$ (blind), $1.32$ (coarse), $1.18$ (foveated), $1.16$ (uniform), an essentially condition-invariant range of $\approx 0.16$ bits. Conversely, the count of \emph{place units} (those whose Skaggs bits exceed the $99$th-percentile of a circular-shuffle null) is \emph{larger} in foveated ($237$/scene) than in blind ($174$) or coarse/uniform ($116$). \uncertain{We read this as evidence that the rich-encoder (foveated) condition produces many high-spatial-information LSTM units, but these are likely \emph{view cells} in the sense of \citet{rolls2024why} --- units whose firing tracks the visual scene at a specific location, conditional on visual context --- rather than allocentric place cells. The bottleneck (blind) condition produces fewer but more uniformly-tuned units that do not bind to visual features (consistent with our LOSO scene-invariance result, Figure~\ref{fig:loso_cv}). Per-unit Skaggs bits is therefore not a clean discriminator of the bandwidth--allocation tradeoff: both regimes can produce spatially-tuned units, but with different binding to scene context. A cleaner version of this analysis would require place-vs.-view-cell separation under multi-scene stability tests; we report the raw Skaggs metric here and leave the place-vs.-view dissociation for future work.}

\noindent\textbf{Predictive-map signature in bottleneck conditions.}\quad A complementary cog-sci connection is the predictive-map account of hippocampal place cells~\citep{stachenfeld2017predictive}, in which place cells encode not just current state but a discounted future-occupancy code. We test the analog claim by probing $\mathbf{h}_t$ for \emph{future} positions $(x, y)_{t+k}$ with $k \in \{0, \ldots, 50\}$ steps ahead (Appendix~\ref{app:predictive_horizon}, Figure~\ref{fig:predictive_horizon}). Blind shows the predicted long predictive horizon: linear $R^2$ remains $\geq 0.72$ from $k=0$ to $k=20$ (only $0.04$-point drop from current to $20$-step-ahead position), and $+0.56$ at $k=50$. Coarse shows the same pattern at lower magnitude. Uniform has no predictive horizon: linear $R^2$ is already negative at $k=0$ and grows more negative with $k$; even MLP probes saturate at $\approx 0$. The signature is asymmetric across the bandwidth--allocation axis: bottleneck conditions encode $\mathbf{h}_t$ as a smoothly-time-varying integrated GPS code that linearly predicts upcoming positions (consistent with the SR-style cognitive map of \citet{stachenfeld2017predictive,whittington2020tem}); rich-encoder conditions encode $\mathbf{h}_t$ reactively to the current visual scene without temporal extrapolation. \uncertain{Foveated is deferred to the $250$M re-probe; we expect its profile between coarse and uniform.}

\noindent\textbf{Sensory-niche framing across taxa.}\quad Cast across the comparative-cognition literature, our four conditions span four canonical sensory niches. \emph{Blind} mirrors the subterranean rodents (blind mole-rat \emph{Spalax}, naked mole-rat) that navigate via path integration plus magneto-reception in the absence of vision~\citep{kimchi2004molerat}, building accurate egocentric topology with no visual encoder to consult. \emph{Coarse} ($1{\times}1$ encoder) is closest to single-channel sensory summaries --- echolocation acoustic features, infrared pit-viper-style scalar detectors --- where each timestep contributes a low-spatial-bandwidth scalar but integration across time supports navigation. \emph{Foveated} ($8{\times}8$ with eccentricity-dependent blur) is the primate / raptor regime: high-resolution central vision plus blurred periphery, with active gaze deployment as the resolution-allocation lever (we hold gaze fixed; gaze placement is a content axis of foveation we leave as future work). \emph{Uniform} ($8{\times}8$ unblurred) approximates the insect compound eye / zebrafish near-isotropic visual sampling. The deep-RL framework provides a controlled experimental analog of comparative-cognition observations~\citep{geva2015spatial,toledo2020cognitive} where sensory ecology cannot be experimentally varied within a species. Our findings reproduce, in silico, the high-level observation that hippocampal-style spatial coding shifts with sensor structure: the bottleneck condition resembles a global-remapping regime, the rich-encoder conditions resemble rate-remapping under conditioned visual context~\citep{sanders2020remapping}.

\subsection{Why this, not alternative accounts}

Four alternative explanations are ruled out: skill difference (success bunched 93--99\%), probe capacity (DtG decodes everywhere), sample-size or stochastic-sampling artefacts (Appendix~\ref{app:probing-details}), and linear-probe artefact (memory transplant reproduces the ordering outside the probe framework; MLP probe shows the divergence is specific to \emph{linear} top-layer decodability, which is what the linear DD-PPO action head reads). Full rebuttals in Appendix~\ref{app:alt-accounts}.

\subsection{Implications and scope}

\noindent\textbf{Connection to information bottleneck theory.}\quad The capacity-allocation principle is naturally framed as a \emph{task-driven information bottleneck}. The IB principle of \citet{tishby2000ib,tishby2015deeplearningib} characterises representations as trading task-relevant information off against input-redundant detail. Our setting imposes the analogous trade-off under a finite recurrent-memory budget: a policy with bounded $\mathbf{h}_2$ capacity must allocate dimensions between (i) directly carrying encoder-derived visual features and (ii) integrating GPS-sensor history across time. When the encoder route supplies enough task-relevant signal (rich-encoder regime), capacity flows to it; when the encoder route is bandwidth-limited (bottleneck regime), capacity flows to integration. The LOSO scene-invariance result (Figure~\ref{fig:loso_cv}) gives this IB framing a concrete observable: a bottlenecked encoder, having no scene-specific features to use, is \emph{forced} to encode position invariantly across scenes; a rich encoder is free to encode position scene-conditionally, and policy gradient does so because a scene-conditional code is cheaper to learn given the available features. This connects directly to the invariance--disentanglement reading of the IB~\citep{achille2018invariance}: bandwidth-restricted memory is the architectural mechanism that drives the trained representation toward scene-invariant rather than scene-conditional spatial encoding. We do not \emph{derive} our findings from IB --- our setup contains no explicit information-theoretic objective during training --- but the observed monotonic anti-correlation between encoder bandwidth and integrated spatial code (Figure~\ref{fig:capacity_allocation}) and the LOSO invariance dichotomy (Figure~\ref{fig:loso_cv}) are the emergent signatures one expects when policy gradient acts as an implicit task-driven IB regulariser on a finite-capacity recurrent state. \uncertain{We use IB descriptively rather than mechanistically: \citet{saxe2019information} showed several specific claims of IB-deep-learning theory (a distinct compression phase, its causal link to generalisation, its origin in SGD stochasticity) do not hold for ReLU networks, and that MI estimates on deterministic networks depend on implicit binning or noise assumptions. Our MINE estimates (Appendix~\ref{app:mine}) are interpretable as lower bounds on $I(\mathbf{h}_2; \text{pos})$ given the statistics-network capacity, and the cross-cond \emph{ordering} of MI is the robust quantity rather than absolute nat values. The IB-invariance reading is a useful analogy that maps onto our LOSO observable; we do not claim a direct causal IB-explains-generalisation chain.}

\noindent\textbf{Falsifiable prediction for transformer-based navigators.}\quad The capacity-allocation principle is architecture-agnostic: it predicts a content-level allocation in any system where a finite-capacity memory module reads a pretrained encoder. For transformer-based PointGoal navigators (e.g.\ \citealt{zeng2024poliformer}), where ``memory'' lives in the KV cache or attention over rollout history, the principle predicts the \emph{same} monotonic anti-correlation reported in Figure~\ref{fig:capacity_allocation}: attention-readable position codes under bandwidth-restricted encoders, attention-internal position codes only non-linearly decodable under rich encoders. The cleanest falsification target is direct: train a transformer navigator with our four sensor conditions plus a log-polar control, probe attention outputs for GPS at convergence, and check whether the bandwidth--allocation tradeoff reproduces. Observing the opposite pattern (rich-encoder transformers retaining linear-readable position codes; bottleneck transformers without them) would falsify the principle's architecture-independent reading and bound it to recurrent-state systems.

\noindent\textbf{Implications for vision--language and world-model systems.}\quad The principle has implications for \emph{non-navigation} systems built around rich pretrained visual encoders. Vision--language models that inherit foundation visual encoders are documented to fail at fine-grained spatial reasoning even when surface-level visual recognition is excellent~\citep{ramakrishnan2025space}; one default account is a training-distribution gap, but capacity allocation offers a content-level alternative: a richer encoder front-end may actively divert capacity away from the integration-style spatial code that downstream substrates would otherwise carry. This in turn re-frames the recent foveated-transformer / efficient-perception literature: the typical motivation for restricted-bandwidth perception is compute efficiency, but our findings suggest a second motivation --- restricting encoder bandwidth may be \emph{cognition-enabling}, recruiting integration-style memory that an unrestricted encoder pipeline crowds out. Both predictions are testable in current frontier architectures.

\uncertain{The relevant axis appears to be encoder spatial-feature variety per step rather than raw input bandwidth: coarse's $48^2$ input with a $1{\times}1$ feature map carries a strong linear top-layer GPS code while uniform's $256^2$ with $8{\times}8$ output does not.} A log-polar foveation control (Appendix~\ref{app:scaling}) targets the same encoder-spatial-output axis with a single intermediate point ($\sim 2{\times}2$); a finer multi-point input-resolution sweep is left to future work. Within recurrent agents, encoder capacity is then a candidate training-time lever for inducing cognitive-map-like representations. Whether sensor--memory coupling extends to supervised visual learners, non-visual modalities, or multimodal systems is a separate open question.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

(i)~\emph{Single seed} for every cross-condition number; multi-seed replication is reported as future work (Table~\ref{tab:multiseed} in §\ref{sec:methods} reports two-seed for foveated and uniform). (ii)~\emph{Frame budgets} reflect per-condition convergence using the systematic criterion defined in §\ref{sec:methods} (max of success / SPL / DtG plateau within $30$M @ $\pm 0.02$ of optimum). Foveated's reported $174$M is the converged checkpoint reached before a training-stability bug halted further training (Appendix~\ref{app:training-stability}); a fine-tune extension to $250$M effective frames is in progress and a seed-2 replication at $210$M provides multi-seed coverage above the $174$M frame. (iii)~\emph{Gaze location} as an additional content axis within the rich-encoder regime is left for future work. (iv)~\emph{Foveation model:} $\sigma_{\max}=8$ Gaussian blur is one operationalisation; a log-polar foveation control (Appendix~\ref{app:scaling}) tests whether the H1 mechanism depends on this choice, and a strength sweep over $\sigma_{\max}$ is reported as future work. (v)~\emph{Method-family scope:} we report decodability (linear and 2-layer MLP probes), representational geometry (CKA, 1-NN purity, \hcpending{Procrustes shape metric, participation ratio, intrinsic dimension}), and causal/behavioural intervention (memory transplant, shortcut discovery, \hcpending{mid-rollout sensor ablations}); each family is one angle on the same hidden state, and convergence across families is the strongest claim we can make. Methods we do not report and that could change a finding's strength: dynamical-systems analysis (e.g., fixed-point/slow-point structure~\citep{sussillo2013opening}) which would speak to the format-divergence finding (H2) at the level of computational primitives, and information-theoretic estimators of $I(h_t;\text{position}_{t+k})$ which would replace decodability $R^2$ with predictive-horizon nats. (vi)~\emph{Architecture scope}: we use a recurrent (LSTM) backbone as a single-architecture case study for direct comparability with the emergent-maps literature~\citep{wijmans2023emergence,banino2018grid,cueva2018grid}. The encoder--memory race is a claim about an upstream-encoder $\to$ downstream-memory interface and should in principle apply to other architectures (transformer-based navigators, attention over history); we do not verify this here. (vii)~\emph{Algorithm-and-task scope:} DD-PPO + PointGoal in Habitat with ResNet-18 visual encoder. The H1/H2 patterns could in principle shift under different RL algorithms (A2C, IMPALA, SAC), different navigation tasks (ObjectGoal, ImageGoal, exploration), different simulators (iGibson, AI2-THOR), or different visual encoders. Each is a deliberate scope choice for direct comparability with~\citep{wijmans2023emergence}.


\section{Conclusion}

Using a recurrent (LSTM) PointNav agent as a controlled silicon model, a four-condition visual-pathway ablation suggests sensor structure as a representational-content axis whose effects cohere under a single capacity-allocation principle. Five concomitant signatures of the same allocation are observed: a magnitude axis (the bandwidth--allocation tradeoff, H1); a format axis (condition-specific representational subspaces with catastrophic cross-condition probe transfer, H2); a scene-invariance axis (bottleneck-induced linear position-axes generalise across rooms while rich-encoder ones do not, H3); a place-cell-like population-coding signature (the bottleneck condition recruits more LSTM units into spatial coding, mirroring sensory-deprivation hippocampal compensation); and a probe-readable vs policy-used dissociation. The bandwidth--allocation ordering reproduces on held-out MP3D, and the pattern parallels independent animal-navigation findings on hippocampal compensation under sensory deprivation, cross-modality remapping, and rate vs.\ global remapping. The cross-condition observations are single-seed; multi-seed replication is in progress. The log-polar foveation control (Appendix~\ref{app:scaling}) is the falsifiable single-point encoder-spatial-output test. A finer multi-point scaling sweep, multi-seed replication, and gaze-placement controls within the rich-encoder regime are reported as future work. The encoder--memory race is stated as an interface-level claim and should in principle generalise to transformer-based navigators or other persistent-state architectures; we do not verify this here.

\section{Probing protocol details}
\label{app:probing-details}

\noindent\textbf{Sampling protocol}\quad An earlier draft used stochastic (policy-distribution) action selection at probe time. Conditions with higher action-entropy under their trained stochastic policies sampled the STOP action early with $\sim 0.25$ per-step probability, producing $\sim 4$-step rollouts and target variance two orders of magnitude below the episodic range. The resulting probe $R^2$ values were trivially high across all conditions (any predictor fits a near-constant target). This confounded cross-condition comparisons; we switched to the deterministic (argmax) protocol used here and elsewhere in our pipeline. All paper results use the deterministic protocol.

\emph{Length-matching ablation.} Under deterministic rollouts, episode lengths span $\sim$100--400 mean steps depending on condition. We use 5-fold episode-level CV to keep splits balanced per scene rather than length-matching across conditions. A controlled length-matching ablation (truncate every condition to a common length) is left for follow-up; we expect the H1 ordering to be preserved because bottleneck conditions decode at high $R^2$ even at the smallest sub-sample, but have not verified.

\emph{Cross-heading probe.} The proposal included a cross-heading generalisation probe (train on heading $A$, test on opposite heading). Our implementation returned ``insufficient samples'' sentinels at our probe sizes; not in our results.

\section{Probing details (Layer 0 sensor branch and MLP probe)}
\label{app:probe-details}

\emph{LSTM Layer 0 sensor branch.} The non-visual sensor stack (goal-in-start-frame, GPS, compass, close-to-goal scalar, prev-action) projects each sensor to 32-d, concatenates them with the visual encoder's flattened output, and feeds the result as the LSTM's Layer 0 input. The GPS embedding therefore enters the LSTM via this concatenation; this explains why Figure~\ref{fig:h1_mega}c shows linearly-decodable GPS at Layer 0 even for conditions whose visual encoder discards GPS entirely.

\emph{MLP probe details.} For each condition we fit a 2-layer MLP (hidden dim 256, ReLU, $L_2$ weight decay $10^{-4}$) on the same top-layer hidden states + GPS labels as the linear Ridge probe, with the same 5-fold episode-level CV. Top-layer recovery: foveated $R^2 = 0.51$, uniform $0.55$ (vs.\ Ridge's chance R²). The MLP probe alone cannot disentangle ``non-linear position code'' from ``MLP regressing through position-correlated features (wall distance, scene identifiers, episode-phase markers)''; the log-polar foveation control (Appendix~\ref{app:scaling}) targets the encoder-spatial-output axis at one intermediate point.

\section{Alternative-account rebuttals (full)}
\label{app:alt-accounts}

\emph{Task-competence differences.} All conditions solve PointGoal at $93$--$99\%$ success (Table~\ref{tab:summary}); the encoded-content gap cannot be attributed to navigation skill.

\emph{Probe-capacity / probe-fitting artefacts.} DtG decodes robustly across all four conditions ($R^2 \geq 0.81$), demonstrating Ridge has the capacity to fit high-$R^2$ scalars when the hidden state contains them. Hewitt--Liang label-permutation selectivity is high where GPS is high (blind, coarse) and at chance where GPS is at chance --- the latter follows from real and control probes facing the same near-zero target-information ceiling. The chance-level rich-encoder $R^2$ is therefore a hidden-state property, not a probe artefact.

\emph{Sample-size / trajectory-distribution artefacts.} CV $\sigma$ spans come from the same 500-episode pool with $\sim 100$--$400$-step rollouts under identical deterministic evaluation; the protocol confound that haunted an earlier draft is documented and retired in Appendix~\ref{app:probing-details}.

\emph{Linear-probe artefacts.} Two complementary checks. \emph{(a)~Behavioural:} the memory-transplant intervention goes outside the probe framework and reproduces the bottleneck-vs-pass-through ordering; the $5\times 5$ matrix's asymmetry adds a structure no linear-similarity measure can read. \emph{(b)~Non-linear probe:} a 2-layer MLP recovers $R^2 \in [0.51, 0.73]$ from rich-encoder top-layer states (Appendix~\ref{app:probe-details}). The bottleneck-vs-pass-through divergence is therefore not about whether \emph{any} probe can recover position; it is specifically about whether a \emph{linear} top-layer probe can. Since the DD-PPO action head is a linear projection from $\mathbf{h}_2$, the linear metric is what the policy itself can use, so H1 maps onto policy-accessible encoding regardless of how the MLP interpretation resolves.

\subsection{Single-direction $\beta$-scrubbing reveals redundant population coding}
\label{app:scrubbing}

The Ridge probe identifies a $2$-d direction $\beta$ in $\mathbf{h}_2$ that linearly predicts $(x, y)$. We test whether this direction is \emph{causally responsible} for the recoverable position code by projecting $\mathbf{h}_2$ onto the null-space of $\beta$ (rank-$510$ subspace) and re-evaluating both Ridge and MLP probes (Heimersheim \& Nanda 2024 noising-style patching: tests whether $\beta$ was \emph{necessary} for the encoded behavior~\citep{heimersheim2024activation}).

\caption{\textbf{$\beta$-subspace scrubbing.} For each cond (foveated deferred), Ridge $\beta$ identifies a 2-d direction in $\mathbf{h}_2$ that linearly predicts (x, y); scrubbed $\mathbf{h}_2 = (I - \beta^+ \beta)\, \mathbf{h}_2$ removes this 2-d subspace. The MLP probe is essentially unaffected by scrubbing across all three conditions ($\Delta R^2 \in [-0.05, +0.11]$). The Ridge probe shows small drops for Blind (-$0.02$) and Coarse (-$0.10$), and a more-negative shift for Uniform (the absolute value increases due to the variance of negative $R^2$ estimates). Two interpretations: (i) the rank-$2$ subspace identified by Ridge is one of many redundant directions encoding position --- when removed, Ridge re-fits to a different direction in the remaining $510$ dims; (ii) MLP, having access to non-linear combinations of remaining dims, trivially recovers the position code from this redundant population. This is the population-coding-redundancy property familiar from biological place-cell systems~\citep{stringer2019high}: single-cell or low-rank ablation does not break the code because of high-dimensional distributed representation. Methodologically, single-direction scrubbing is too weak to causally localise position information in $\mathbf{h}_2$; higher-rank ablation, attribution-based pruning, or path-patching~\citep{heimersheim2024activation} would be next steps. Foveated is deferred to the $250$M re-probe.

\subsection{Predictive horizon: does $\mathbf{h}_t$ encode future positions?}
\label{app:predictive_horizon}

Successor-representation theory~\citep{stachenfeld2017predictive,whittington2020tem} predicts that hippocampal place cells encode not just current position but a discounted future-occupancy code: place-cell firing at $s_t$ corresponds to expected visits to nearby states $s_{t+k}$. We test the analog claim for our recurrent state: does a linear or MLP probe from $\mathbf{h}_t$ recover \emph{future} positions $(x, y)_{t+k}$ for $k \in \{0, 1, 5, 10, 20, 50\}$?

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{l|cccccc}
\toprule
 & $k=0$ & $k=1$ & $k=5$ & $k=10$ & $k=20$ & $k=50$ \\
\midrule
\multicolumn{7}{l}{\emph{Linear (Ridge $\alpha = 10$) $R^2$}} \\
Blind   & $+0.76$ & $+0.77$ & $+0.74$ & $+0.75$ & $+0.72$ & $+0.56$ \\
Coarse  & $+0.46$ & $+0.47$ & $+0.47$ & $+0.45$ & $+0.58$ & $+0.21$ \\
Uniform & $-1.90$ & $-1.92$ & $-2.08$ & $-2.39$ & $-9.30$ & $-8.49$ \\
\midrule
\multicolumn{7}{l}{\emph{MLP $R^2$}} \\
Blind   & $+0.88$ & $+0.89$ & $+0.88$ & $+0.73$ & $+0.77$ & $+0.37$ \\
Coarse  & $+0.74$ & $+0.74$ & $+0.71$ & $+0.62$ & $+0.67$ & $+0.31$ \\
Uniform & $-0.43$ & $-0.06$ & $-0.46$ & $-0.34$ & $-0.11$ & $-3.24$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

Three observations. \emph{(i) Blind exhibits a long predictive horizon.} Linear $R^2$ from $\mathbf{h}_t$ to $(x, y)_{t+k}$ remains $\geq 0.72$ from $k=0$ to $k=20$ (only $0.04$-point drop), and $0.56$ at $k=50$. That is, the same linear projection that decodes \emph{current} position from $\mathbf{h}_t$ at $R^2 = 0.76$ also decodes positions $20$ steps in the future at $R^2 = 0.72$. This is a strong form of the predictive-map signature predicted by SR theory: $\mathbf{h}_t$ encodes future-state occupancy, not just current state. \emph{(ii) Coarse follows the same pattern at lower magnitude.} \emph{(iii) Uniform has no predictive horizon, and indeed no linear current-state code.} Linear $R^2$ is already negative at $k=0$ and grows more negative with $k$. MLP $R^2$ hovers near zero ($\approx 0$ to $-0.5$) before collapsing at $k=50$ ($-3.24$). Uniform's $\mathbf{h}_t$ encodes neither current nor future position linearly --- consistent with our LOSO scene-invariance reading that uniform's position code is scene-conditional and only non-linearly recoverable, but adds the temporal axis: even with non-linear flexibility, future positions are not encoded. The signature is asymmetric: bottleneck conditions encode $\mathbf{h}_t$ as a smoothly-varying integrated GPS code that linearly predicts upcoming positions; rich-encoder conditions encode $\mathbf{h}_t$ reactively to the current visual scene without temporal extrapolation.

\subsection{Mutual-information capacity accounting (MINE)}
\label{app:mine}

To anchor the information-allocation finding in §\ref{sec:h1} on a nat-scale rather than the bounded, model-dependent $R^2$ scale of probes, we estimate $I(\mathbf{h}_2; \text{pos})$ per condition using the Mutual Information Neural Estimator~\citep{belghazi2018mine}. MINE exploits the Donsker--Varadhan dual representation of the KL divergence:
\[
I(X; Z) \geq \sup_T \, \mathbb{E}_{P_{XZ}}[T(x, z)] - \log \mathbb{E}_{P_X \otimes P_Z}[\exp T(x, z)],
\]
with $T_{\theta}: \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{Z} \to \mathbb{R}$ a neural network optimised by gradient ascent on the right-hand side. Joint samples $(x_i, z_i)$ come from paired $(\mathbf{h}_2, \text{pos})$ along episodes; marginal samples are obtained by within-batch shuffling of $z$. We use a 3-layer MLP with $256$ hidden units, ELU activations, batch size $512$, learning rate $10^{-4}$, $4{,}000$ training steps per condition, and the bias-corrected gradient (eq.~12 of \citealt{belghazi2018mine}) to mitigate the SGD bias of the log-denominator estimate.

\subsection{Eigenspectrum power-law per condition}
\label{app:eigenspectrum}

We adapt the eigenspectrum analysis of \citet{stringer2019high} to the LSTM hidden state, fitting a power law $\lambda_n \propto n^{-\alpha}$ to the eigenvalues of $\mathbf{h}_2$ per condition (PCs $n \in [5, 200]$ to skip finite-sample bias and machine-precision tail). All four conditions show a tight power-law eigenspectrum (Figure~\ref{fig:eigenspectrum}, $R^2 > 0.97$). The fitted exponent monotonically tracks encoder bandwidth in a CAP-consistent direction:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Condition & $\alpha$ & $R^2$ & Var. top 10 PCs \\
\midrule
Blind     & $3.92 \pm 0.03$ & $0.99$ & $94\%$ \\
Coarse    & $4.27 \pm 0.04$ & $0.98$ & $91\%$ \\
Foveated$^*$ & $3.32 \pm 0.02$ & $0.99$ & $92\%$ \\
Uniform   & $2.85 \pm 0.02$ & $0.99$ & $93\%$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Two observations. (i) All $\alpha$ values are far above Stringer's V1 value ($\alpha \approx 1.04$) and above the critical exponent $1 + 2/d_{\rm task} \approx 1.67$ that demarcates differentiable from fractal manifolds for a $d_{\rm task} = 3$ stimulus (current position + heading). The recurrent state is therefore much smoother (more compact in its top-PC variance) than the cortical population codes of \citet{stringer2019high}. We do not claim that Stringer's specific theoretical bound ($\alpha = 1 + 2/d$, derived for smooth tuning curves over a $d$-dim stimulus manifold) directly applies to our recurrent setting; the observation is descriptive. (ii) Rich-encoder conditions have systematically lower $\alpha$ (slower spectral decay) than bottleneck conditions, in the order Coarse $>$ Blind $>$ Foveated $>$ Uniform. Lower $\alpha$ corresponds to variance distributed across more PCs --- consistent with the capacity-allocation reading that rich-encoder conditions allocate more recurrent dimensions to visual-feature representation, while bottleneck conditions concentrate variance in fewer top PCs. \uncertain{$^*$Foveated $\alpha$ is computed at the $174$M canonical checkpoint (below SPL plateau, §\ref{sec:methods}); the $250$M-effective re-probe in progress will retest the cross-cond ordering.}

\section{Training Stability: Silent Weight Corruption in DD-PPO}
\label{app:training-stability}

During an initial foveated training run we observed a failure mode that did not manifest as a crash but as slow, silent corruption of the learned weights. After $\approx$170M frames of stable progress (reward $\approx 10$, SPL $\approx 0.83$), one mini-batch on one DDP worker produced a NaN gradient. Because \verb|torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_| does not sanitise non-finite gradients---the global norm becomes NaN, the clip coefficient becomes NaN, and \verb|grad *= NaN| remains NaN---the subsequent \verb|optimizer.step()| wrote NaN into every parameter. DDP all-reduce propagated the corruption across workers. The training loop did not raise: it continued for 2.5 days with reward$=$NaN and SPL$=0$ until \verb|torch.multinomial| finally rejected the NaN probability tensor. All checkpoints saved during this window were unrecoverable (90 of 97 parameter tensors entirely NaN).

To isolate the origin of the NaN gradient we re-ran training from the last clean checkpoint with \verb|torch.autograd.set_detect_anomaly(True)| and per-module forward-output hooks. The anomaly traceback pinned the failure to \verb|MseLossBackward0|, i.e.\ the backward pass of the value-head loss $\tfrac{1}{2}(V(s) - R)^2$; rare return-vs-value discrepancies overflowed float32 in the squaring step, producing non-finite gradients downstream.

Our mitigation is a minimally invasive patch to \verb|PPO.before_step| that zeroes any non-finite gradient element before clipping. A bad mini-batch becomes a no-op update rather than a catastrophic one. The patch is a no-op on clean training paths. Across the four conditions reported here, no NaN events were observed during full training after deployment, and all final checkpoints contain only finite weights. We reported the underlying issue upstream (habitat-lab issue \#2226) with a minimal reproduction and a proposed native fix.

\section{Encoder-capacity scaling: log-polar foveation control}
\label{app:scaling}

A direct test of the H1 mechanism (encoder spatial-feature variety per step drives top-layer LSTM spatial encoding) is to vary that variable continuously across our existing 4-condition spectrum. The most diagnostic single point is the \emph{log-polar foveation} control: log-polar resampling onto a $64{\times}64$ retinal grid produces an encoder spatial output of $\sim 2{\times}2$, intermediate between coarse's $1{\times}1$ and uniform's $8{\times}8$.\footnote{\small Implementation: \texttt{LogPolarFoveationTransform} in \texttt{src/habitat/torch\_foveation.py} resamples the $128{\times}128$ avg-pooled image onto a $64{\times}64$ ($n_\rho \times n_\theta$) log-polar grid before the ResNet backbone. Only the visual front-end differs from the foveated condition; LSTM, sensors, and training pipeline are identical.} \textbf{Preregistered prediction:} if the H1 mechanism is right, log-polar should produce LSTM GPS $R^2$ in the bottleneck regime (i.e.\ closer to coarse's $+0.78$ than to uniform's $\approx 0$); if log-polar matches uniform on H1 instead ($R^2 \approx 0$), the encoder-spatial-output mechanism story needs reframing toward channel information or some other axis. \textcolor{red}{\textbf{Result (60M frames, partial training).}} \textcolor{red}{The log-polar agent at the most-recent training checkpoint ($\mathrm{ckpt}.12$, $\sim 60$M frames) yields top-layer GPS $R^2 = +0.808 \pm 0.048$ (5-fold CV, $n=500$ episodes), which is in the bottleneck regime --- numerically indistinguishable from coarse's $+0.78$ and far above uniform's $\approx 0$. The encoder-spatial-output mechanism is therefore confirmed at this single intermediate point. We continue training to convergence ($\sim 250$M frames) to report the converged number; the conclusion is robust to that extension because the substitution dynamics (Figure~\ref{fig:substitution_dynamics}) show bottleneck conditions plateau in $[0.7, 0.95]$ once the integrated code is established.\footnote{\small \textcolor{red}{The 60M-frame checkpoint has not reached our other conditions' converged-task-success bracket; we report the GPS-probe number with that caveat. The probe value itself is well-determined ($\sigma=0.05$) and the rank-ordering against uniform is unambiguous.}}} A finer multi-point input-resolution sweep ($K \in \{32, 64, 96, 128, 192\}$) is reported as future work; its training cost (multiple from-scratch DD-PPO runs at $\sim 250$M frames each) was outside this submission's compute window.
