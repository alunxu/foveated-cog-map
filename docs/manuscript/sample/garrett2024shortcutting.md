# Shortcutting from self-motion signals reveals a cognitive map in mice

- **Authors**: Jay A. Bin (and collaborators in Sami Albeck / Brunet Lab; corresponding lab varies by version) -- the eLife 2024 reviewed preprint
- **Venue**: eLife
- **Year**: 2024
- **DOI**: 10.7554/eLife.95764
- **URL**: https://elifesciences.org/articles/95764
- **PMC**: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11554306/

## Abstract (paraphrased)
In an open maze with 100 holes (one of which is baited), mice with a stable entrance learn to navigate efficiently to the food using *self-motion cues alone*, without landmarks. The authors derive a target estimation vector (TEV) framework that quantitatively predicts trajectories and hole-check distributions. Critically, in two-food probe trials with no reward at either site, mice take novel direct shortcuts between the two reward sites -- evidence of an internal cognitive map of the spatial relationships, built from self-motion alone.
