Abstract

The format of spatial memory in the hippocampus varies systematically with a species' visual ecology, but whether this reflects a general sensor–memory coupling is hard to test directly: an animal's sensor ecology is fixed within its species, and AI systems rarely co-train an encoder and a memory across a graded sensor design. We test this in silico, in a navigation agent where only the visual sensor varies—along a graded scale of encoder bandwidth—while architecture, task, and training are held fixed. Across five conditions the agents reach near-identical task performance, yet their hidden-state code for position differs in form: with a vision-limited encoder, position concentrates into a scene-invariant direction that is linearly readable from memory; with a rich encoder, it distributes into non-linear directions and partly offloads to the encoder. Linear readability shifts faster than total position information—a format shift, not a collapse of information. We frame this as an emergent capacity allocation between an encoder route and a memory-integration route, measured along three concomitant views: magnitude, format, and consumption. Memory transplants are asymmetric—rich-encoder policies fail on bottleneck states while the reverse works—paralleling cross-modality remapping in hippocampal coding, and probe-readable position dissociates from policy reliance. Together this positions sensor structure as a training-time lever on cognitive-map format, yields a falsifiable architecture-agnostic prediction for transformer navigators, and reframes bandwidth-restricting perception as potentially cognition-enabling rather than only compute-saving.

Introduction — Motivation

In artificial intelligence, visual perception and downstream memory are commonly treated as a sequential pipeline, guided by the implicit assumption that scaling the visual encoder uniformly improves downstream memory representations. We instead question this underlying assumption: how does the structure of a sensor constrain the spatial information accessible from memory? Cognitive neuroscience has long recognized that sensory ecologies inherently shape the format of cognitive maps. For example, foveated primates rely on view cells[1], near-panoramic rodents depend on place cells[2], and congenitally blind humans show striking cross-modal remapping[3],[4]. Because biological evolutionary histories cannot be held constant, empirically isolating the causal role of sensor structure in nature is notoriously difficult. We bridge this gap in silico, utilizing deep reinforcement learning agents to rigorously evaluate how systematically varying visual sensor constraints impacts the fundamental format of spatial memory.

Introduction — Hypothesis

We hypothesize an emergent capacity-allocation principle: an agent's visual encoder and its recurrent integration of continuous motion history (e.g., GPS) actively compete for a finite hidden-state memory budget. An agent can build its spatial representation via two distinct computational routes: integrating motion over time to develop a cognitive map (which works even without vision), or reading position-correlated visual landmarks from the current scene (which only works with rich vision). We predict that as visual bandwidth increases, the agent's internal representation will actively reallocate capacity away from the motion-integration route and heavily tilt toward the visual route. By varying only the visual sensor structure while keeping the core agent architecture fixed, we expect the underlying format of the resulting cognitive maps to diverge entirely.

Related Work

Spatial reasoning in modern vision systems. Vision–language models with strong visual recognition still fail at fine-grained spatial reasoning[17]. Existing accounts locate the problem either in the training distribution or in the geometric content of pretrained encoders—and, tellingly, linear probes show that encoders often contain the relevant geometry while the downstream model fails to use it[16]. Rather than treat perception and memory as a sequential pipeline in which a bigger encoder is always better, we ask how the structure of the sensor constrains what spatial information is accessible from memory through a simple linear readout.

Sensor ecology and cognitive-map format in biology. Comparative neuroscience has long recognized that distinct sensory ecologies drive distinct spatial codes: foveated primates rely on view cells[1],[2] while near-panoramic rodents rely on place cells, and echolocating bats and congenitally blind humans show striking cross-modal remapping[3],[4]. A unifying account frames such reorganization as remapping under hidden-state inference, where place fields reconfigure when sensory evidence supports distinct latent states[9]. Isolating the causal role of sensor ecology is notoriously hard in biology, however, because body plans and evolutionary histories cannot be held constant across species.

Emergent cognitive maps in artificial agents. Deep-RL navigators develop cognitive-map-like representations even in the absence of vision[11], echoing the emergence of grid- and place-like codes in artificial agents trained for spatial localization[20],[21], and such memories carry a predictive, successor-representation structure[10]. We build directly on this line—using the canonical Habitat / DD-PPO PointGoal pipeline[5],[6]—but add the missing ingredient: a single, systematically varied degree of freedom (encoder spatial bandwidth) and a convergent systems-neuroscience probing toolkit (linear and non-linear probes, mutual-information estimation[14], leave-one-scene-out validation, subspace geometry, and causal memory transplants) applied to a controlled comparative population of agents rather than a single recording.

Our comparative testbed isolates visual bandwidth as the sole variable. We evaluate five conditions spanning a graded scale of visual bandwidth: blind (no vision), coarse (48x48 pixels collapsed to a 1x1 bottleneck), foveated (eccentricity-dependent gaussian blur), foveated-logpolar (retinal grid), and uniform (unblurred 256x256). Crucially, all five conditions share an identical ResNet-18 encoder backbone and a 3-layer LSTM[6],[11], and all are provided with the exact same non-visual sensor stack (goal, GPS, compass, previous action), ensuring that any divergence in memory stems purely from the visual input. Agents were trained from scratch using DD-PPO[6] on a PointGoal navigation task[5] for a 250M-frame budget across a merged pool of 3D environments[7],[8]. Following training, the weights were frozen and the top-layer hidden state (h₂ ∈ ℝ512) was collected step-by-step from fresh, out-of-distribution rollouts across 18 held-out test scenes. We then read this frozen memory along three complementary views of a single capacity allocation—magnitude (how much linearly-readable position survives), format (what shape that code takes, spatially and temporally), and consumption (whether the policy actually uses it)—using a convergent battery of linear and non-linear (MLP) probes, mutual-information estimation, leave-one-scene-out validation, subspace geometry, and causal memory transplants. Following standard practice in comparative-cognition modeling[11], we train a single seed per condition and instead derive rigor from this multi-method convergence.

Disclaimer: Although the agents in this study do share the same architecture for the encoder (and memory), their weights are in fact co-trained with the policy which means that, throughout training, the different agents will effectively end up developing different "encoders" since the weights will end up being different because of the differences in the inputs.

Before examining the agents' internal memories, we first evaluated their behavioral competence on the PointGoal navigation task. Despite receiving substantially different visual inputs, all five agent conditions successfully learned to navigate and reached near-identical task performance. Success rates across the conditions were at ceiling, ranging from 93% for the blind agent to over 99% for the rich-encoder agents.

While all agents consistently reached their goals, visual bandwidth did impact their path efficiency. Sighted agents—even the heavily bottlenecked coarse condition—navigated highly efficient routes, achieving Success weighted by Path Length (SPL) scores between 0.85 and 0.89. The blind agent, relying solely on integrated motion history, successfully navigated but took less optimal paths, resulting in an SPL of 0.59. Crucially, because all conditions converged behaviorally and achieved near-perfect success rates, the profound differences we observe in their internal memory representations cannot be attributed to a simple gap in navigation skill; rather, they reflect a true representational dissociation.

Result 2 — Magnitude

Figure 2a — Magnitude — what does h₂ encode?

Figure 2b — Substitution mechanism — when does GPS get crowded out?

Our first view of the allocation reveals a fundamental trade-off: the linearly-readable position signal at the policy-readable memory layer (h₂) shrinks monotonically as encoder bandwidth increases (Figure 2a). The blind and coarse agents display high linear decodability for absolute position—their integration route dominates the linear position code—whereas the foveated and uniform agents show a near-complete collapse. Because all five conditions sit at near-ceiling success (Table 1), this is a representational dissociation, not a skill gap.

The driver is not raw output dimensionality but per-step feature variety. The foveated-logpolar control, whose encoder emits only a ~2×2 feature map—nearly as small as coarse's 1×1—nonetheless lands squarely in the rich-encoder regime, placing the relevant variable on the diversity of visual features delivered each timestep rather than cell count. This trade-off also emerges during training: all sighted agents start with high linear readability, but rich-encoder conditions decay as the policy gradient learns to substitute internal integration with external visual landmarks, while bottleneck conditions stay stable throughout.

Crucially, the collapse is not a loss of information. A control confirms the linear probe is capable—it reads distance-to-goal at R² ≥ 0.81 in every condition—and a MINE mutual-information estimator[14] shows total spatial information barely moves (blind 6.01 bits, coarse 4.55, uniform 4.47). Rich-encoder conditions retain the position information; they simply encode it non-linearly. The collapse is therefore a format shift, not an information collapse.

Result 3 — Format

Figure 3b — Format dichotomy

The magnitude collapse of Result 2 left an ambiguity: when the linear position code vanishes, is spatial information lost, or merely reformatted? We already know it survives—a non-linear probe recovers it and total mutual information barely moves—so the collapse is a format shift. The plot on the right isolates the sharpest cut of that shift: not how much position lives in memory, but in what kind of representation it lives—a portable, world-anchored map, or a scene-specific one.

How to read it. Each marker is one condition, placed on two orthogonal properties of its memory. The horizontal axis is scene-invariance, measured by Leave-One-Scene-Out (LOSO) cross-validation: we train a linear probe to decode a spatial axis from the hidden state on all-but-one held-out room, test it on the room it never saw, and average over every left-out room. A high LOSO R² (right of the 0.5 divider) means a single position direction generalizes across rooms—the agent carries one shared, world-frame map. A low LOSO R² (left) means the position direction re-orients from room to room—the code is scene-conditional. The vertical axis is transplant cost, a purely behavioral, probe-free measure drawn from the 5×5 memory-transplant experiment: we splice one condition's recurrent memory into the navigation loop and record the resulting SPL penalty. A low cost means the memory is in a format another policy can actually consume; a high cost means the code is idiosyncratic and breaks when moved. The two axes cross-check the same question—"is this a universal map or a private one?"—from a decoding side and a behavioral side. The desirable "true cognitive map" corner is bottom-right (scene-invariant and transplant-robust); the top-left corner is scene-conditional and brittle.

What it shows. The split is not graded—it is binary. The blind agent sits alone in the bottom-right corner (LOSO R² ≈ 0.9, transplant cost ≈ 0.03): one linear, scene-invariant map that transfers to unseen rooms and that other policies can navigate with. Every condition with any vision collapses into the top-left cluster (LOSO R² ≈ 0.2–0.4, transplant cost ≈ 0.13–0.18). Crucially this includes coarse—the very condition whose linear position code was nearly as strong as blind's in Result 2. So scene-invariance does not track magnitude: the boundary here falls cleanly at vision-on versus vision-off, not along the graded bandwidth scale. And because the LOSO (decoding) and transplant (behavioral) verdicts agree, the dichotomy cannot be written off as a quirk of the linear probe.

What it means. A fully linear, scene-invariant cognitive map is not a property that degrades gracefully with bandwidth—it requires removing vision entirely. The moment per-step visual evidence is available, the agent stops maintaining one allocentric world frame and reorganizes its position code per scene: the in-silico analogue of hippocampal global remapping[9], where place fields reconfigure when sensory evidence supports distinct hidden states. The transplant asymmetry behind the y-axis sharpens this—blind-style memory can drive any condition's policy, but rich-encoder memory only drives its own, mirroring cross-modality remapping in biological navigators[3]. The implication for AI and biology alike: a vision-deprived agent does not hold a lower-fidelity copy of the sighted map—it holds a categorically different representation, and rich encoders do not blur the cognitive map so much as replace it with a scene-bound one.

Result 4 — Temporal Stability

Figure 4c — Future-position predictability

Format is temporal as well as spatial. Vision lets an agent anchor to a fixed, stable map; without it, the blind agent must recompute its location from self-motion at every step, so its internal code continuously rotates—it rebuilds the map step-by-step. A temporal-generalization analysis makes this concrete: a decoder trained at one timestep only reads nearby timesteps for the blind agent (a diagonal stripe), whereas sighted agents' decoders generalize across the whole episode (a stable square). Consistently, the blind agent shows the largest cumulative change in its hidden state per step, and its code's stability decays fastest with time-lag.

This continual integration leaves a forward-looking signature (Figure 4c): only the blind agent sustains a deep predictive horizon, linearly decoding its future position many steps ahead—the successor-representation cognitive-map signature[10]—dropping only 0.04 in R² between decoding its current step and 20 steps into the future, while sighted agents cannot. A map actively integrated from motion and a map statically extracted from vision are categorically different computational objects: blind units even integrate over an intrinsic timescale 2.1–2.5× longer than every sighted condition, reproducing a cortical-hierarchy slow-integrator gradient within a single recurrent network.

Result 5 — Consumption & Causal Validation

Figure 5a — Readability ≠ policy use

A linear probe reveals what memory stores, but not what the policy actually relies on. To close this gap we use a paired shortcut protocol[15]: an agent finishes an episode, is reset to its start, and is given a new goal in the same scene while retaining its recurrent memory; the SPL penalty of keeping that stale memory (versus wiping it) measures how much the policy leans on it. Readability and reliance dissociate in two telling cases. Coarse carries a readable linear position code yet barely relies on it ("readable but unused"), while uniform has no linear position code yet suffers the largest penalty ("unused but readable via a substitute")—its memory encodes scene identity more strongly than position. In trajectory overlays, only uniform's persistent agent locks onto the previous episode's goal (+1.83 m closer to the old goal than the new one); blind, coarse, and foveated still head for the new goal.

We then ground the format shift causally, outside any probe, with asymmetric memory transplants: blind-style memory can drive any condition's policy, but rich-encoder memory only drives its own—mirroring biological global remapping under cross-modality reorganization[3]. Two further controls characterize the mechanism: re-initializing memory from a previous end-state drops SPL comparably across all five conditions (memory here is an activity-slot store, bound to trajectory dynamics rather than weights), and ablating the GPS+compass sensors collapses success everywhere—confirming the position sensor as the upstream causal source that the integration route encodes.

Discussion — Synthesis & Biological Parallels

Rather than treating magnitude, format, and consumption as independent phenomena, we synthesize them as three views of a single underlying principle: the visual encoder and the integration of the position sensor actively compete for a finite memory budget, and the encoder's bandwidth tilts the balance. Bottleneck conditions retain a linear position code, rich-encoder conditions reallocate to the visual route, and the policy reads from whichever route the memory makes available. The reading is geometric: a finite memory has a fixed number of dimensions to spend. When an encoder delivers rich, near-complete spatial information at every step, memory has no reason to maintain a separate linear position code and commits those dimensions elsewhere; when visual bandwidth is heavily restricted, memory must reconstruct position by integrating motion over time, forcing the code into a form the linear policy head can consume—exactly what the invariance–disentanglement reading of the information bottleneck predicts.

This artificial phenomenon strongly mirrors biological precedents in comparative cognition, such as cross-modality remapping in echolocating bats and congenitally blind humans[3],[4]. Furthermore, we found this capacity-allocation principle to be architecture-agnostic. By porting our experiment to an entirely different environment, Memory Maze[12], using a different visual encoder family, frozen DINOv2 patch features[13], we successfully replicated the exact same format-shift dynamics. This confirms that the principle governs how any finite-capacity memory interacts with sensory structures, bridging artificial intelligence with the systems-neuroscience toolkit.

Conclusion

A five-condition visual-sensor ablation in a recurrent PointGoal agent establishes sensor structure as a representational-content variable: holding architecture, task, optimization, and reward fixed and varying only encoder bandwidth, all agents converge behaviorally yet diverge internally. The effects cohere under a single capacity-allocation principle, read through three concomitant views—magnitude (the linear position code shrinks as bandwidth grows), format (the surviving code shifts from a linear, scene-invariant world map to a non-linear, scene-conditional one, and from a slowly-anchored to a continuously-rebuilt temporal code), and consumption (what the policy actually relies on dissociates from what memory stores). Asymmetric memory transplants ground the format shift causally, outside any probe, and the whole pattern reproduces in a different environment and encoder family—evidence the principle acts at the encoder–memory interface rather than being an artifact of one pipeline.

The cross-condition signatures are at a high level consistent with independent animal-navigation findings—hippocampal compensation under sensory deprivation, cross-modality remapping, and the no-/global-remapping axis under hidden-state inference[9]—which we frame as an open bio–AI hypothesis rather than a demonstrated parallel. Beyond the specific result, we view the study as a step toward a comparative cognitive neuroscience of artificial navigation agents: a programme in which matched-condition populations of trained agents are analyzed with the very metrics used to map biological cortex and hippocampus, in a setting where the relevant variables can be held fixed.

Future Work

These findings have broad theoretical implications, reframing bandwidth-restricting perception (like foveation) not merely as a compute-saving measure, but as potentially cognition-enabling. Because our capacity-allocation principle acts at the interface between the encoder and memory, it generates a falsifiable prediction for modern AI architectures: we predict this same format shift will appear in transformer-based navigators.

Most provocatively, our results offer a candidate mechanism for the spatial-reasoning bottleneck in state-of-the-art vision–language models (VLMs). That bottleneck is increasingly traced not to faulty perception but to a failure to consolidate and decode spatial structure: vision encoders capture geometric detail that language decoders fail to translate into accurate outputs[16]; generating discrete text tokens forces models to approximate continuous space, causing numerical drift[19]; models struggle with egocentric-to-allocentric transformation and rarely build the spatial mental models humans use to track unseen objects[18]; and, tellingly, linguistic Chain-of-Thought prompting can even degrade spatial reasoning[17]. Our capacity-allocation account suggests why: when a rich encoder supplies near-complete features at every step, the downstream substrate is never pressured to consolidate a linearly-readable, scene-invariant cognitive map—so there is no clean map left for a decoder to read. This reframes the design goal. Rather than scaling the encoder, an architecture whose memory is induced to build an internal cognitive map—which we find bandwidth-restricting designs like foveation and log-polar sampling actively encourage—should reason more readily about the geometric and semantic relationships in a scene. The strongest external evidence is concurrent: VLMs trained to "map-then-reason"—to emit an explicit cognitive map before reasoning over it—recover much of the lost spatial accuracy[18]. We frame this as a falsifiable hypothesis, not a demonstrated result: our agents are recurrent PointGoal navigators, and porting the five-condition design to VLM-scale architectures is the natural test.

Methodologically, while our single-seed experimental design aligns with standard comparative-cognition modeling, future work will expand to multi-seed variance grids and finer continuous visual sweeps. Ultimately, this paves the way toward a "comparative cognitive neuroscience of artificial navigation agents," applying the analytical toolkits used on biological brains directly to AI.
